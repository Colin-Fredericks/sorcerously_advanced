Worlds

The setting of Sorcerously Advanced starts with the civilizations of the Great Basin, but it extends far beyond them. This chapter details not only the physical geography of the Basin, but the mirror-worlds of the Kaleidoscope and the more metaphysical realities of the Dream Realms and the Worlds Beyond.

This chapter gives a broad overview of each region, describes how it works, and picks out a few specific places of interest.

Cosmology

The center of all things is Aion, whose names are Eternity and Epoch and The Turning Of Ages – Aion the Unalterable, the Great God whose life is the universe.

In the world where humanity lives, which we call the Great Basin, Aion is a human figure, gold and androgynous, so tremendous that the million-koss-wide Great Basin sits comfortably in two of Aion’s many hands.

This world is one of many, collectively called the Kaleidoscope. Each world has its own lands and seas, its own Aion, its own sentient peoples who resemble their creator.

If the Kaleidoscope is the universe of matter, the Worlds Beyond are a universe of thought and energy. Each one has its own patterns, its own gods, and its own people, but they also act as afterlives for the people of the Kaleidoscope.

Dream Realms too are worlds of thoughts, but they are the fleeting and changeable thoughts of people, not the weighty and substantive thoughts of Aion. While the Worlds Beyond exist outside of the Kaleidoscope, the Dream Realms exist within and between living, thinking beings. They are ever-present and ever-changing.

All these worlds are not separated by distances that one can walk – or fly. If the nearest world in the Kaleidoscope is within the Great Basin’s universe, it is so distant that it cannot be seen. One moves between worlds using magic or not at all. In the Worlds Beyond, some realms sit side by side, and one can indeed walk from one dimension to another, but this is not the rule.

There are oddities, too. Where is the Red Space located? Is the Transit Lattice one of the Worlds Beyond? Even those who study cosmology have, thus far, found no answers to these questions.

Flow in Other Worlds

The Kaleidoscope and the Worlds Beyond produce flow, usually at the same rate as the Basin.

The Dream Worlds exist within and between the minds of the living. They produce flow, but that flow is always aspected with the character of the world. A nightmare-realm would produce terror-aspected flow, while one more contemplative might produce flow with a puzzle-aspect or a planning-aspect.

The Travel Lattice is a -1 Null Zone. Flow does not enter the Lattice directly, but instead continuously leaks into it through a vast number of access points.

The Red Space extinguishes flow at a prodigious rate and produces none of its own.

People, Outlanders, and Gods

When we talk about “people” in this chapter, we mean anyone with sapience and volition. Any inhabitant of the Kaleidoscope is a “person”, whether they are human or sentient tree or Ixitxachitl.

When we use the term “outlander”, we mean someone from beyond the Kaleidoscope – typically a denizen of the Worlds Beyond. Outlanders are sapient but have constrained volition.

When we use the term “god”, we mean a part of the world – any world – that is a sentient responsive process. Gods have little to no volition. They can rarely act in ways contrary to their nature. If they do, they lose much power and are sometimes replaced. They are often unaware of this. Most gods of the Basin sleep until people interact with them, after which they might or might not remain awake for a time. The gods of the Worlds Beyond are more active, and are the focal points of their worlds.

The Great Basin

Most of this game takes place in The Great Basin, a dish-shaped world of earth and water that is humanity's home. The Basin is a million koss in width, which makes its surface area over 15,000 times that of the planet Earth.

In the perfect center of the Great Basin is the Hub, a mountain range where the weather is serene and the flow is smooth like calm waters. This is the First Continent, where humanity is presumed to have originated billions of years ago, before they spread out into the world.

The Basin has all manner of terrain, both mundane and supernatural. There are forests, swamps, mountains, plains, and jungles. There are oceans and islands, volcanoes and waterfalls. There is more ocean than land, but not by much. Nearly every continent is inhabited.

Regions of hot and cold are well-separated, but rely more on the presence of elemental wellsprings than on the incidence angle of light. Beneath the world, gemstones, veins of metal, and volcanic materials are slowly but constantly being generated and returned to nothingness, just as the flow passes through the air in ley lines.

As one moves outward toward the Rim, more fantastic terrain becomes more common, as the ley lines carry great rivers of aspected flow through the world. There are fields of crystalline extrusions, mile-deep crevasses, sparking towers of bronze and glass, forests of color, hills of iron, and clouds solid enough to live on. Null Zones likewise become more common, and the rule is “feast or famine” – lands are either flooded with strongly aspected flow, or nearly devoid of it.

The civilizations described in the previous chapter are located about halfway between Rim and Hub. They stretch for perhaps seventy thousand koss in either direction, covering a mere speck in the Basin’s vastness.

The Basin's atmosphere extends upwards for about a thousand koss until it thins out. There above the world one can see the calm flow of mountain-shattering power in the Attic Lines, ley lines driven upward and out of a chaotic atmosphere, into a void where they could flow quiet and safe.

The Basin has one sun and several moons. Eclipses of the sun are not one passing before the other, but the Eye of Aion rotating to face the world and peer at its surface. Eclipses of the moons are moments of great magical potential, where the element of the Void is temporarily ascendant.

Beyond the Basin is airless space. This void stretches for as far as anyone has ever investigated. It is nearly empty (save of course for the body of Aion), but it is also so vast that there are still a myriad of unusual things to be found floating in its depths, most of them relics of previous civilizations.

Places of Interest

When deciding where to send your intrepid Archivists next, you may want to consider the following locales:

The Black Sand Desert

One of the best-studied supernatural locations in the Basin, the Black Sand Desert is a stronghold for the Cult of the Empty Grave and was once the center of a massive, continents-spanning empire.

Ancient homes carved into cliffs ring the desert. In its depths, broken buildings lie half-buried in sand. The death-aspected sand pulls in light, and travelers must bring light sources with them. Instead of cacti, dead trees like bleached driftwood grow short and stunted on the tops of dunes. Caravans cross the desert to collect rare reagents. The Archive is often active here, seeking the origins of the desert itself and learning the history of this long-lost empire.

The Clockwork Cathedral

The so-called Clockwork Cathedral is a city recently pulled from the past by the Worldbuilders. Its gears and counterweights speak of a time of mechanical acumen and grand architectural works.

The Worldbuilders have permitted others to access the Cathedral – the Archive for historical research, a Diadem family attempting to duplicate its workings, a cult of Nova racing them to the same goal, Those Above seeking to harness its unique alchemical flow. Now the Worldbuilders seem to regret their decision, and each group lobbies that they might be allowed to remain.

The Colossus

In the distant past, someone tried to build an Aion in miniature. It was not entirely successful... and yet it did not entirely fail, either. A golden colossus strides the lands rimward of Diadem, a full koss tall, long and steady of gait. Their upturned hands hold towns, and cities have been built into their back.

The colossus acts as an amplifier for calendrical magic, its steps and heartbeat tuned to astrological timescales. This makes it a tempting trophy for many civilizations. The Geometers hold it now, but the Unroyal slip in and out of their dreams and their allies in the Hearth-kin plan to oust them before the next eclipse lets them fortify their wards and solidify their power.

The Garish Sea

Each wave a different color, the Garish Sea is both stunning and and treacherous. Several nations encircle the sea, each with their own name for it: the Rainbow Sea, the Prismatic Ocean, the Blinding Waves, the Motley, Aion’s Paints.

The animals of the Garish Sea provide both rare unconjurable food and potent reagents. Diadem plies these waters for travel and fishing, with the Unroyal snapping at their heels. Glimmermere seeks ways to preserve the fantastic colors for their artwork.

The Hollow-eyed Towns

Scholars of the Archive believe that an alien god sleeps in this place, perhaps fallen from some other part of the Kaleidoscope. Dreams are filled with inhuman language that the True Tongue does not speak. Mirages arise with wavering towers that taper like tentacles. Walk toward them and they vanish. Conjuration too becomes strange; the local Consensus bears things with no discernible purpose, familiar to no one.

There are six small city-states here, their people often sunken-eyed from lack of restful sleep. Despite the atmosphere, they seem pleased with the way things are and do not wish to leave. Newcomers are quite unlikely to stay.

The Sky-Lands

Weather in the Basin generally moves Rimward, following the flow. Some clouds circle crosswise to this, ever spinward. Those with the eyes of an eagle might notice people walking about on those clouds.

The term “Sky-Lands” is used for any number of nation states or city states that live on solid clouds in the Basin’s sky. They farm strange windblown plants, drink water collected as they pass sideways through nimbus and stratus, burn their dead and scatter the ashes. The Ægis seeks the secrets of these floating fortresses, while the Hearth-kin attempt to educate them and the Sovereigns seek fugitive demons hidden here for untold years.

The Tilting Hills

Gravity in the Basin points downward.... usually. There are occasionally times when this fails to be true, where waterfalls move upward or a mountainside feels strangely flat. In the Tilting Hills, gravity skews from time to time, and visitors must grab hold of something and fight vertigo lest they fall queasily to the ground.

The Geometer’s Guild researches the Tilting Hills for their odd measurements. Glimmermere visits often for the strange and vertiginous perspective. The Worldbuilders are the only people seeking to colonize them. With few people barring their way, they have been “restoring” them to flatness with alacrity this past century.

The Underneath

Not everyone in the Basin lives above it. Some live within, and some live even below, in the Underneath. Whole new civilizations wait to be discovered in this place, people who have never seen the sun nor the moons. The Groundbreakers’ efforts and Nova’s explorations have barely begun to uncover the secrets of the Basin’s underside.

As mentioned above, gravity in the Basin points downward. This is still true in the Underneath. The people here are not pulled upward toward the Basin. Instead, buildings hang from chains or are carved into mountainous stalactites. Water seeps through the stone above. Life here must cling to the ceiling or fall thousands of koss to Aion’s inconceivably large, glowing hands beneath.

Windfall

Windfall is a quiet place at the ends of the world, a pocket of calm between roaring ley lines, vacuous Null Zones, and the emptiness beyond the Rim. A slow breeze blows always from hubward, carrying the occasional leaf over the edge.

In this peaceful space a monastery has been built to provide Archivists with a place to rest their bodies and minds. A dozen or so live here at any given time, not counting the healers and staff who care for them.

Nonhumans in the Basin

Humanity is by far the dominant intelligent species in the Basin. There are communities of other beings, but each one traces its origins back to elsewhere in the Kaleidoscope. Likewise, there are communities of human beings elsewhere in the Kaleidoscope, and even farther away. There is much conjecture that parts of the Kaleidoscope occasionally intersect, that magic was common in the past as well, or that the Travel lattice can be accessed by accident more easily than one might think.

The Kaleidoscope

The Great Basin of that the Archive calls home is one world of many. These are known as the Kaleidoscope, for their close resemblance to one another.

Each world’s reality is similar: there is flow, there is magic, there is the Gift, gravity holds one down. Each is different in subtle ways: aspected flow, constant winds, new colors. The differences are rarely so great or pervasive as to prevent individuals from one world from surviving in another, but there are some worlds with poisonous water or choking, sulfurous air.

The Kaleidoscope is either very large or perhaps infinite. No end to it has ever been found. The Travel Lattice extends into every part of it – or perhaps there are worlds beyond the Lattice’s reach that have yet to be found for their lack of connection. Thus far the Archive has catalogued over 700 worlds.

The Great Basin is held in the hands of the Great God Aion. Other worlds of the Kaleidoscope find themselves similarly cradled by one or more manifestations of Aion, which look somewhat like the intelligent species of that world. On one, Aion is a moth; on another they resemble a bipedal tiger; on still another, an elephant. One Basin sits on the back of a great golden stingray with a million eyes, for the people who live there are intelligent rays. Another thirty-mooned world is held in the belly of a golden-haired sloth with the deepest and softest of fur, and lo and behold the people there are slothlike in their appearance. Still another is gently supported above the heads of three tentacle-faced beings, their wings folding and unfolding in mystical patterns. It has no sun. Some Basins are empty of intelligent life, and there Aion seems sad for that lack.

The arrangement of the worlds in the Kaleidoscope is a matter of much theory and little evidence. Are they stacked like a deck of cards? Arranged in a constellation or some grand symmetric pattern? No one knows, and there seems to be little way to tell.

Worlds of the Kaleidoscope

The Archive typically names worlds in the Kaleidoscope for their inhabitants – the local name nearly always translates to “The Great Basin”, after all. Traveling through the myriad of possibilities, one might run across...

Batoidea

The Basin of Batoidea is one of the sights recommended to new Archive recruits. Nestled on the back of a golden manta ray, it shimmers with deep waters and beautiful archipelagos. The people here live more in the shallows, though the Gift has empowered them to explore the cold, dark depths as well.

Batoidea is well-settled by visitors from other worlds. It seems that cracks in the Lattice and wild-magic whirlpools deposit the unlucky here more often than on other worlds.

Corvidae

The people of Corvidae are the greatcrows. Four meters from beak to tail and black of feather, their Basin is held in the talons of an Aion shining and clever.

The era of wars after the Gift was not kind to the corvids. Their small civilizations turned against one another in wars like they had never known. Their remaining settlements are small and widely spread. They have little trust of one another.

Cephalopoda

This is one of many Basins that is primarily sea, with little land. The people of Cephalopoda are octopi, with bodies the size of human torsos and long tentacles.

Until the Gift, the Cephalopods were short-lived, dying not long after procreation. The so-called “Interregnum” never happened on this world. Instead, the immortality of the Gift allowed them to finally form longer bonds. The last 400 years have seen them finally build the sorts of civilizations seen on other Basins.

Lepidoptera

The people here are moths and butterflies. Their bodies average a meter long, with each wing half again as wide. Their colors span every hue in the rainbow and many beyond it. Aion beats its wings every seven years, flying through the endless void toward a single distant star.

The Lepidopterans are homebodies by nature. Travelers are rare. Flowers are numerous, and the people had what they needed even before the Gift. They made few empires. All this is to say that the age after the Gift was milder here, and Lepidopteran historians have a longer memory because of it.

Loxodonta

The Loxodons are elephant-folk, smaller but still massive, some leather-skinned with great ears, others shaggy with fur to keep out the cold. They have always been proud of their wayfaring heritage, and took the Gift as an opportunity to begin traveling en masse once again. Their larger civilizations have faded away, replaced by smaller and more local tribes.

The Basin here is made of wood rather than stone. It is ever-growing, naturally renewing, and pieces of it break off the Rim and drift out into the void. Some Loxodons have recaptured these to act as vessels of exploration.

Orichalca

There are few worlds in the Kaleidoscope whose primary inhabitants are so clearly artificial. The Orichalcans are not only made of metal, and not only cog-and-springwork automata, but each is also stamped with a serial number and a date of manufacture (though in an unknown date system).

Whether the Orichalcans were made to resemble Aion in this world, made to resemble the inhabitants, or whether they are the original inhabitants is unknown. No new Orichalcans have been created in such a long time that all memories of the old days have been overwritten.

Rodentia

Rodentia is a world with multiple intelligent species, unique for their small size. Whereas most people in the Kaleidoscope are roughly human-sized, the people of Rodentia are sentient mice, rats, and voles of the size that would fit in a human hand.

The Basin here is likewise tiny compared to the Basin of humanity, but it is honeycombed with minuscule tunnels, and the populations are similar. This makes flow a carefully rationed resource. Wars are fought over ley line placement. Rodientians are perhaps the most likely people to leave their Basin, simply for the breathing room.

Tarsiidae

Close enough to human to be unsettling, the people of Tarsiidae are large-eyed, long-fingered, and possessed of short fur instead of hair. They are more dexterous than humans, but physically weaker and slightly smaller.

The Basin of Tarsiidae is crisscrossed with ancient tracks. Some ancient civilization replaced steel with indestructible cuendillar, but not before the world became accustomed to a thin layer of rust. While the wars after the Gift damaged some of them, there are still more than enough intact to run a vast train network. Several local civilizations are extending that system into the Travel Lattice.

Vulcanae

The Basin of Vulcanae is one of the most hostile known. The surface is perpetually molten, with a few cooler places where it has solidified. The air is sulfurous, gas pockets ignite frequently, and tremors are common. The first Archive expedition to Vulcanae found their immortality sorely tested.

The local people are well-adapted to their world. Though humanoid in form (head, torso, two arms, two legs), they are themselves made of molten rock. They live on the “shores” of islands with cooler temperatures, and near the Rim where the cold void of space provides the same. Their magical studies are somewhat impaired by the difficulty of writing in their environment.

Do other worlds have tiny humans?

Most of the sentient creatures of the Kaleidoscope can be found in the Great Basin, albeit in smaller and non-sapient forms. The greatcrows of Corvidae, for example, find the Basin’s “ordinary” crows freakishly fascinating. Humanity-analogues can likewise be found in other worlds, typically as a knee-high primate with relatively little hair.

Aquatic Peoples and Travel

The Travel lattice is not made to be swum.

This is problematic for the people of Cephalopoda and Batoidea. Magical solutions (such as shapeshifting or spirit-swimming) are made more difficult by the fact that the Lattice is a Null Zone. Thus far there has been no safe solution.

The Vulcanians likewise have difficulty. In their case, it’s because the Lattice is so much colder than their native habitat. Bridges to Vulcanae occasionally bear a basalt-and-pumice statue, the sad remains of a Vulcanian who tried and failed to reach another world.

Reaching New Worlds

Travel through the Lattice is targeted by one’s memories and understandings. How, then, does one reach a world one has never seen?

In this age of the world, it is most common to be brought by a friend. Failing that, one finds an ancient writing (in physical form or in the Universal Repositories) with true descriptions of a world upon the Kaleidoscope or one of the Worlds Beyond.

It is possible to simply invent a place in one’s mind and open a door, hoping that the destination exists. This will, more often than not, deposit you in a place you vaguely remember – the location that inspired your imaginings. For Dream Travel, though, this is the default method.

The Dream Realms

Within and between the minds of all living beings are the Dream Realms. They are invisible but ever-present. Every thought and feeling connects to them, every nightmare spawns a new one or rejoins an old one. Without thinking beings they shrivel and might even disappear, though naturally it is difficult to observe such events. Some Dream Realms are so large that there is always someone lost in them, some poor unfortunate soul whose mind and presence maintains them.

While the Worlds Beyond seem different to each sentient species, two visitors to a Dream Realm will agree in every detail on what they saw. This is not to say that the Dream Realms are more comprehensible. These places are allegory made tangible. They make sense as metaphors, not as real-world places.

Because each realm is tied to an emotion or thought, there are often signifiers of those things here, drawn from the subconscious of visitors. If they associate rage with fire, the Dream Realm of rage will ignite around them. If they associate rage with ice, it will chill or freeze. One can learn – or reveal – a lot about oneself in the Dream Realms.

Dream Realms may seem infinite, but in truth they are simply creating what an explorer imagines might be present. Those with the art of Dreamshaping can control this; others are at the whim of their own subconscious. If they secretly expect an edge to the world, they shall find it; if not, they may travel forward eternally.

Entering the Dream

Visiting the Dream Realms is done via the art of Dream Travel. One can travel in physical form, or in a purely mental form also known as astral projection.

Those who travel physically run the risk of being disoriented by the local conditions. They also need to use magic to return themselves to the Basin, and can potentially be imprisoned.

Those who travel astrally will find themselves changed by the feel of the place – less disoriented, more transmuted. They can snap themselves back at any time with just a thought, but their bodies lie asleep or comatose in the Basin while they travel.

Dream Travel and Spirit-walking are akin to one another. Someone spirit-walking in astral form can slip in and out of dreams as easily as walking to the next room.

Regardless of how one arrives, actions in the Dream Realms are taken with Communion, Mystery, and Trickery. The more physical Natures have no sway here.

Dream Realm Inhabitants

There is nothing in the Dream Realms but what its visitors bring with them. Every being you see in a Dream Realm is part of your own consciousness, or the mind of someone nearby. Killing them does nothing. They may appear again at any moment or lie dormant, as appropriate to your own thoughts. Objects and places are drawn from memories, often warped or accentuated by emotion.

It is rare but possible for figments from a Dream Realm to escape. They take the original thought with them. If your longing for someone takes on physical form and leaves to pursue them, you will no longer yearn. They are a part of you, but they are gone.

Many Dream Realms are said to be sentient, but in truth they are merely amalgams of their visitors.

Dreaming Whilst Dreaming

It is possible to sleep while in the Dream Realm, and even to dream. One can slip from one Dream Realm to another this way. Astral visitors who fall asleep instead return to their bodies.

It is not possible for a dream traveler in physical form to mentally project themselves back to the Basin – only into other Dream Realms. It is because of this that the citizens of Glimmermere are particularly cautious when visiting the Basin. They are used to walking in mental form from one realm to another, but when they visit the Basin they wager their bodies.

A Catalog of Dreams

A small sampling of Dream Realms can be found below.

Aion's Shadow

The feeling that one is not at home in one’s own house, that one’s family is not who they seem. Aion’s Shadow is a dark mirror that touches our own world and replicates it in ill-remembered detail. This realm is used for Spirit Walking by certain Traditions.

Berit'tuni

The obsession with number and arrangement. Here all things are discrete and separable. Stones rise faceted from the ground. Shapes with impossible angles fall from the sky and one cannot help but count them.

Fiasha An

Unrequited longing. The moon hangs forever in the sky. The land is positively littered with scenic overlooks and lone trees under which to sigh over loves that cannot be. Soft shapes move silently and peacefully in the mist.

Inwolath

The joy of swimming. Inwolath transforms all who enter it into fishlike forms so they can swim in its currents and rest in its waters.

Khai'Gholam

The nightmare from which dreamers cannot wake. It is the terrifying dream that clutches one and strikes over and over, unending. Those who visit in astral form leave their bodies shaking and sweating.

Nunto Karak

A broken realm of dreams interrupted. Waves of nonexistence sweep the world, sweeping away structures and inhabitants. Only visitors remain unchanged when they pass.

Qli-thak-kos

A cycle of thought, in which all tends towards its opposite until roles and hearts are reversed, and then flows back. One must use caution when deciding when to leave.

Sunset-Over-Sea

The peace of a rose-tinted sky. This is where Glimmermere went when its residents left the Basin. Many still live here now, and while they protect themselves from the realm’s emotional effects, it does provide a beautiful and quiet refuge for students of the arts.

Ushendi-loves-Bendar

The drive of affection and new-formed attraction. Crushes and infatuations find ways to make themselves known here. This is a realm to make young lovers blush.

The Worlds Beyond

The Worlds Beyond are places outside of the Kaleidoscope. They have a reality and permanence that the Dream Realms lack. A Dream Realm with no visitors withers and disappears; the Worlds Beyond persist.

This is because of the source of these worlds. Whereas the Dream Realms reflect the thoughts and emotions of living beings, the Worlds Beyond are part of the mind of Aion. Each world expresses a process or complex within Aion's mind. In many ways, the Worlds Beyond are Aion's mind, too vast to be contained by its body.

Most of Aion's mental processes seem to map well to the human mind. Some do not. Sometimes one can easily tell what the concept is, as with the literally fiery passion of the Brass Inferno or the paranoid Net of Response. Other times it's not as clear.

If one thing is clear from the resonance between these worlds and Aion’s self, it is that Aion’s mind is not perfect. Psychologically speaking, Aion may not even be mentally well. As much as Aion has self-love, there are heavens. As much as Aion has self-hating thoughts, there are hells.

Each of the Worlds Beyond are centered around one or more supernaturally powerful beings. These gods are always strongly linked to the thoughts of the realm. They have no choice but to express them in one way or another.

These gods created (and sometimes still create) the other inhabitants of their respective worlds, collectively referred to as outlanders. Each is a hybrid between the realm’s core thought and other concepts. Their societies portray certain structures-of-mind within Aion. Outlanders are beings of constrained volition, unable to step beyond their roles, like actors in a perpetual play.

Each World Beyond revolves around the relationship between creators and the created. Some gods bring outlanders into being to worship them, or to seek their favor. Others generate outlanders to build endless structures, or to explore existence and report back. The interactions between gods, outsiders, and souls from the Kaleidoscope says volumes about the state of mind of the local god.

Speaking of souls: the Worlds Beyond are also afterlives. If the Uncountable Spinners of Silver Cord did not exist, then souls from the Basin would go immediately to the Worlds Beyond, and eventually leave them to reincarnate, free from the memories of their past lives and their post-death experiences. The Spinners, broken as they are, change this. Some souls reincarnate immediately. Some stay in the Basin for much longer than others. Some remember pieces of their pasts.

Which afterlife one arrives in is not reliably predictable. Most people go to afterlives their people knew from times long past. Many people are judged by the standards of their cultures. None of this is absolute. The process is broken.

The Worlds Beyond sometimes touch the Basin, but more often must be reached via Travel. Each of the Worlds Beyond has one and only one place that connects to the Travel Lattice. Sometimes there might seem to be multiple gateways to different “realms” in a single world. In truth, these are places where multiple worlds have been stitched together so that natives, visitors, and perhaps the souls of the departed could walk between them.

Each person sees the Worlds Beyond through their own lens. Local idioms are translated into personal understandings. When human beings visit the Worlds Beyond, the gods and their servants seem humanoid (though other visitors retain their own appearance). When creatures from elsewhere in the Kaleidoscope visit, they fit the form of those visitors instead. The Sixth Sense can reveal these forms as false, if one thinks to check, but not everyone does. This makes tomes found in the Universal Repositories untrustworthy. If a ruler is described as a titanic varicolored snail, check to see whether the author was Muricodean.

The Gift in the Worlds of the Gods

Kaleidoscope natives who were in the Worlds Beyond at the time of the Gift did not receive the capability for magic. Only the portion that broke magical bonds was passed to them. It is not known why. Some worlds still have enclaves of humans who know nothing of the Gift, the local gods being too possessive or mindless to tell them.

As fragments of Aion’s mind, the gods of the Worlds Beyond are not eligible for the Gift. They did not receive it; they cannot be given it.

Unlike the gods, however, outlanders are hybridized with other ideas. Whether one considers these creatures less conceptually pure or more human, they are nevertheless distant enough from Aion that they could receive the Gift. This would provide them with free will and allow them to decide whether to stay in their world or move beyond it.

This last fact not common knowledge. Convincing outlanders to leave their roles for long enough to complete the required ritual is difficult.

A Register of the Beyond

Here is a small sampling of the Worlds Beyond. Some of these entries are categories; others are specific locales.

Elemental Planes and their kin

The five Elemental Planes are infinite expanses of infinite variations on the components of the world. Each is ruled in its own way: the Maelstrom Conquerer of Water, the Void Council, the All-Consumption group-mind that rules Fire. Traditions whose Fount is Elementalism often draw their power directly from these worlds.

Around these planes lie dozens more worlds touched by those elements. From the sky-realms full of airships to a dark underground of lava and dwarves, each reflects its own blend of take on the elements. Many shade into emotional resonances as well – the Brass Inferno is a sensual realm where arousal generates literal heat. Others were connected to or created by a particular Kaleidoscopic world. The Grand Chasm, for example, might once have been like the Plane of Earth, save that an entire civilization mined it for a billion years and then left only their dwellings behind.

The Forever City

This multifaceted metropolis was once linked via Travel to a thousand destinations across a thousand worlds. Then whatever supplied its flow ran out. Now the doors here only work sporadically, and the city is full of fugitives and squatters. The city-gods are still strong in this place, and their outlanders sustain the workings of the city to keep the place’s remaining flow feeding its pantheon.

Heavens

Places of peaceful rest and recuperation. The best-known heaven is the once-vacant heaven that now serves as the headquarters of the Golden Ægis. It is a sky-realm of granite floors and marble pillars, empty tables and lonely thrones.

Not all heavens are vacant. Most, in fact, are inhabited by gods and angels, with varying amounts of interest in the Kaleidocope and matters there. While their involvement with the outside world varies, the common thread is compassion. Heavenly pantheons seek to help others, especially the souls that have come to them from the Kaleidoscope.

Hells

As there are many heavens, so are there many hells. The one intertwined with the Sovereigns of Hell is perhaps the most famous, but many of the Basin’s peoples have stories of souls treated harshly in the “next world”.

Each hell is uniquely different. Some burn, others freeze, still others overwhelm with gore until no shock remains. Some have tortures tailored to the individual, some do not. Some resemble heavens until the awful moment of revelation comes.

If the thing that unites all heavens is compassion, the common factor for all hells is torment – and that they are notoriously difficult to escape.

The Museum Labyrinth

Worlds such as this one appear to be Aion’s memory. Here, artwork showcasing previous ages of the world are kept in a museum with glass walls. The god here is eager to show what has happened, though it rarely focuses on important events, preferring those about which it remembers unending detail.

Escape from the Museum Labyrinth is difficult. There are dozens of explorers still wandering its halls, seeking, seeking... something...

The Fool's Scriptorium

Not all of the Worlds Beyond are vast. This one is tiny, composed of a single shed-sized room. Every space is filled with scrolls, and every scroll is filled with nonsense. The god of this place laughs eternally.

The Net of Response

The Net’s inhabitants maintain a spy network stretching across large portions of the Worlds Beyond. This is because the god of this place desires to know. It must apprehend all things, and then it must decide on the proper response to them, quickly. The faster this happens, the calmer the realm. When information is slow to come, the god becomes agitated, and its servitors rush from place to place – often ineffectually.

Deciding on a response doesn’t mean acting on it. The decision only need be made, not executed. Then records can be made, and the world can move on to the next point of data.

Stranger Places

There are places that do not fit easily into the cosmology described above. Many of these show signs of having been artificially created, remnants of some past civilization’s long-lost magical works.

The Travel Lattice

The art of Travel opens giant doors into an airy void full of bridges. Every bridge is exactly five koss from end to end. Their width varies, but they are generally wide enough for at least two wagons to pass. Some of them have ornately carved railings; others are bare spans of marble.

They pass in all directions, up and down, left and right, sometimes one back-to-back with another. One can see no sky or ground, only an endless random mesh. It is difficult to determine much else about this world but that it is filled with bridges. They are not indestructible, but they are quite hardy, and damage seems to be repaired when no one is looking.

While it is not unheard-of for fellow travelers to pass within sight of one another in the Lattice, it is uncommon. The void is vast, and the number of bridges inconceivably large. If the Kaleidoscope is infinite, the Travel Lattice is even more so.

The Red Space

The Travel Lattice has some unfortunate properties. It doesn’t generate flow, you can’t grow food there, its local Canon of Form is nothing but bridges. Some people live there, but it’s not a wonderful existence.

Next to the Red Space, however, it is positively welcoming.

The Red Space seems actively hostile to living beings. It generates no flow of its own. Any that is produced, such as inside an unlucky visitor, simply falls away and disappears as if swirling down a drain. It is grating to every sense: a penetrating red glow from all about, a buzzing sound that one can never get used to, a smell universally disliked, even a gritty texture on the fingers and tongue. One can find no rest in sleep. The ground grows nothing, the sky has no clouds.

Despite all this, the Red Space preserves nonliving matter well. The passage of time does not ravage anything within, at least, not physically. Food stored here will not rot, books will not molder. Its vastness, at a scale of over sixty thousand to one with the Kaleidoscope, provides virtually unlimited space.

Horror stories are told of people wandering the Red Space, driven slowly insane by the light and isolation, hungry for the slightest dribble of flow and the possibility of returning home. The Soulpact has as one of its goals the rescue of all Red Space exiles, but as of yet they have no good way to locate them.

Local Demiplanes

There are many smaller worlds attached to the Kaleidoscope in various places. Some ancient peoples built soul repositories with their own tiny afterlives. Others carved out vacation spots or hideaways. The GM is encouraged to create small worlds as desired, or as the rich and powerful would have desired, to fit their needs.

Hazards

The Great Basin can be a dangerous place, even for magi. The danger is further compounded when they explore beyond its borders. This section covers some of the natural and magical threats that can be found in various parts of the Basin and other worlds.

Static Hazards

These include environmental conditions, flow abnormalities, traps, and automated defenses. They are typically handled using the Four Questions, with the following Natures and Professions relevant:

Rockslides, volcanoes, other immediate disasters: Industry or Self 2, and the Samaritan profession

Storms, cold/heat, physical conditions of Warped Flow Industry or Self 2, Explorer/Healer

Warped Flow conditions, emotional or moral: Communion or Self 2, Clergy/Courtesan/Politician/Prolocutor

Warped Flow conditions, perceptional or intellectual: Mystery or Communion 2, Artist/Medium/Scholar/Spy

Traps: War 2 or Self 4, Soldier/Spy/Theurge

Automated defenses: War or Trickery 3+, Soldier/Spy/Theurge

Static hazards are a fact of life for some parts of the Basin, but they’re rarely a major part of the story. You overcome them or you figure out a way around them, and you move on. All the real dangers in the Basin are alive.

Living Hazards

Dangers posed by living beings are handled using the Conflict system, but with a limited set of game stats.

Some of these creatures have a conflict timescale listed that’s different from the usual “a few seconds”. It’s tough to fight an entire plague of locusts at once, or to punch out a koss-long serpent with a fist just a few inches wide. If you need to settle things more quickly, treat this like a Project in terms of spending Reserve to speed things up.

All living hazards should be assumed to have a Core Value of 4 in their central areas of competence, and 0-1 elsewhere. These “virtual” CV ratings represent instinct rather than morality. All the creatures on this list are non-sapient. While some are clever, none of them can hold a conversation with you. As soon as you’re dealing with something you can talk with, it should get a full character writeup.

Challenged to a Dance-Off

In the unlikely event that you force one of the creatures in this section into a different sort of conflict than its Professions would cover, any nonexistent Natures are 1 and Professions are 0. The creature retains its Core Values, including the usual Self-Preservation 4.

Getting these creatures out of their comfort zone is pretty hard to do. Attempting to debate The Blue City’s End or challenge The Ones With All The Teeth to a riddle contest will probably end with them just trying to kill you.

Giant Beasts

Ivory Thunder is an elephant the size of a hill. She is an example of a Gargantua. Gargantua are a rare but “natural” phenomenon (in as much as “natural” has meaning in a magical world) wherein otherwise ordinary creatures grow without limit throughout a lengthened lifespan. She acts very much like an ordinary elephant – she travels with a herd of (much smaller) elephants, and she is fairly smart for an animal. Gargantua eat more than an ordinary animal, but not as much as one might imagine.

Natures: Self 4, War 3

Professions: Battle 3, Shelter Others 2, Weather the Elements 4

Infrastructure I

Conflict timescale: 10 minutes

Rimward Current is a koss-long whale-serpent that lives half in our world, half in a state of perpetual spirit-walking. Its species, scattered throughout the Kaleidoscope, are very curious but not intelligent enough to communicate beyond basic concepts. Rimward Current is mostly a danger to aquatic construction projects that are sizeable enough to draw its attention.

Natures: Mystery 3, Self 4, War 3

Professions: Disappear into water 4, Eat 3, Investigate 2, Shrug it off 5

Infrastructure II

Conflict timescale: 30 minutes

Hordes

The Blue City's End is the name for a horde of billions of self-replicating tin locusts. The Blue City was a haven for magi a thousand years ago, until everything went wrong. Its strange periwinkle building materials repaired it. Its placement in the mountains far from any ley lines sheltered it. The Blue City Alchemists were well-known among magical circles, and they gathered mountains of books and scrolls in their libraries. The city's defenses were a bit sparse, and one of the magi was worried. They built a half-dozen tin automata, tiny but fierce, and gave them the power to reproduce like living beings. They destroyed everything.

Natures: Self 2, Trickery 3, War 4

Professions: Overwhelm 3, Hide 3

Infrastructure I

Conflict timescale: 10 minutes

The Timeless Burrowers are massive psychic worms linked in horrible dreams. They live many koss beneath the surface. They are forever asleep, but speak to one another in dreams. Sometimes they feel the tug of the living above them, and their minds rise to scratch the flea that has bothered them off the Basin's back. The Burrowers may be from another world in the Kaleidoscope, or perhaps the Great Basin hosts more than one intelligent species, but they live on such a timescale that communication is all but impossible. Words take years, sentences take centuries. Strike against them, though, and they will flail at you until you are crushed in mind and body.

Natures: Communion 4, Self 4, War 5

Professions: Burrow 5, Devour 4, Nightmares 5

Infrastructure II

Conflict timescale: 1 week, mostly because of the difficulty in actually reaching them.

Flow Parasites

Flow Parasites eat magic. They’re the exception to the rule that you can’t steal someone else’s flow or flat-out prevent someone from casting spells. When running conflicts with Flow Parasites, it’s ok to apply Complications that interfere with spellcasting ability, but only in the specific ways listed below (“Flow Complications”). These are generally resisted with Self.

Most Flow Parasites are roughly human-sized. They’re vaguely human-shaped, or perhaps bear-shaped, or like some kind of poorly made half-eaten scarecrow. They are all fractals of dimension from 2-4, somewhere between “flat” and “all too solid”.

The Ones With All The Teeth: In their mouth, down their throat, on their hands, on their faces. The Ones With All The Teeth are unsubtle holes in the world with gleaming white edges of enamel. They live along ley lines in remote locations where they have not yet been driven out, but would much rather eat something with more variety. They drag their prey back to hiding places, stashing them together so that when one’s flow is stagnant they can suckle from another. Their victims are usually found in shreds, slowly attempting to regenerate.

Natures: Self 5, War 5

Professions: Eat Your Magic 5, Slaughter 4

Conflict timescale: normal

Flow Complications:

  • Trivial: A painless tickling sensation
  • Minor: A few minor cuts that leak flow
  • Moderate: All the flow of one Nature leaks out of you through a nasty wound. You can’t access it until the wound heals substantially.
  • Major: As Moderate, except for it’s either War, Self, and Industry or Communion, Mystery, and Trickery that you’re locked out of.
  • Critical: Incapacitated by the pain as a torrent of flow is ripped from your corpus.

The Ones That Follow You Home slide invisibly into shadows. They only take magic from those who are alone. They'd prefer to leave their victims unaware that anything even happened, so they'll try to steal it while you sleep. They'll come back night after night if they can. Victims typically lose Mystery first, to become less aware, then Communion, so they can't call for help, then War so they can't fight back. Put up a fight and they're much more likely to leave than fight back. Once they’re in, though, they live in your shadow and draw bits of magic like a tick draws blood.

Natures: Self 3, Trickery 5

Professions: Ambush 3, Hide 3, Steal Your Magic 4

Conflict timescale: normal

Flow Complications:

  • Trivial: A chill
  • Minor: Out of breath
  • Moderate: Take -1 to Mystery, Communion, or War (cumulative as long as it’s attached)
  • Major: You’re inclined to explain away anything else it does. Treat future conflicts as Subtle automatically, with no penalty.
  • Critical: It’s permanently attached to your shadow, as if you were always in a -2 Null Zone.

The Ones Without Eyes take sensory magics first. They’re among the weaker flow parasites, which is fortunate: they exist in large numbers in the deep wilderness. They are able to find food easily. Taking a bull’s sense of smell or a bird’s proprioception will feed them for days. Before they breed, they feast, taking a every sense from as many animals as they can find and spawning hundreds of eggs like holes where someone has erased the world. Few of them make it to adulthood as they steal each others’ senses and leave their broodmates to starve helplessly to death.

Natures: Self 3, Trickery 3

Professions: Stalk 3, Steal Your Senses 3

Conflict timescale: normal

Flow Complications:

  • Trivial: That wasn’t you blinking. This isn’t tinnitus.
  • Minor: You can’t smell or taste anything. You become dizzy or lose track of time.
  • Moderate: Your sense of hearing or touch is gone. You lose arts like Psychometry.
  • Major: You’re blind. You lose your sixth sense and Farspeech.
  • Critical: You are entirely without sensory input and cannot interact with the outside world. Even Rapport is denied to you.

Personhood

We’ve tried to make it clear, but it bears repeating: There are no “intelligent monsters” in this game. There are mindless monsters, and there are sapient beings capable of choosing their actions. If you can converse with it, that’s not a monster, that’s a person.
