Game Play

This chapter gives you the rules for playing a game of Sorcerously Advanced. These rules are the way in which you and your character interact with the universe of the game.

There are two ways to get things done in this game. The first way is through the use of character-level abilities, such as Core Values, Nature scores, and Expertise. The first part of this chapter deals with character-level actions. The second way is through the use of player-level abilities, such as Approaches and Plots. Player-level abilities are available only to the players’ characters, and not to GM characters. The second part of this chapter deals with player-level actions.

Both character-level and player-level actions are tied into Complications and Advantages, which represent story events in game terms. Your character’s statistics are the cause; Complications and Advantages are the effects. Because they are so important and so central to the game, we’ll start by discussing them and then move on to other game mechanics that tie into them.

Difficult Play and Safety Tools

This game revolves around themes of...

  • The use and abuse of power
  • Political, cultural, and academic colonialism
  • What we become when we are limited by our desires and beliefs rather than our minds or bodies

Any or all of these may result in emotionally difficult play, particularly where they overlap with players’ real-world experiences, and especially for those who have had bad experiences with abuses of power in their lives. To improve the play experience for everyone, there are three rules that apply while you are playing Sorcerously Advanced:

  • The Biology Rule: Anyone can leave the play space at any time, for any reason, or for no reason at all, with or without explanation, with no penalty. The GM or players can inquire if they’re concerned – “You ok?” “Did something happen?” – but no response or clarification are required.
  • The Good Friends Rule: GMs and players gladly accept the task of adjusting their stories to avoid causing pain or disgust. All participants are mandated to respond to indications of concern or revulsion, such as: "Ughhh", "Um...", "Ew", "Oh lord", "Hold on", "Where is this going?", "Can we not?". Any channel is acceptable: verbal, note passing, text messages, facial expressions, a raised hand, etc. The response should be immediate, and might include: a time-out to discuss with the group, a private conference, or simply asking what to do. Good phrases to remember include “Are we ok?” “Should I wind that back?” “Should we skip this?”
  • The Self-Determination Rule: Let others determine their own levels of comfort. If you read pain or disgust in someone’s manner, bring it up – but if they tell you they’re ok, let the matter rest. Everyone has their own reasons for playing RPGs.

These are fairly unstructured rules, are intended to work within the social norms found in many gaming groups. Our hope is that more “natural” interactions will make it easier for people to bring up when they’re uncomfortable before that turns into pain. If you’d like a set of tools with a bit more structure to them, we recommend starting with the Tabletop RPG Safety Toolkit, a free resource.

Complications and Advantages

Sorcerously Advanced revolves around Complications and Advantages, collectively known as Effects. In order to understand the rest of the game system, you need to understand Effects.

Complications are bad things that happen to your character, or unfortunate circumstances that surround them. Being injured, losing money or prestige, and being kidnapped by your opponents are all Complications.

Advantages are the reverse: favorable circumstances and good things that happen to your character. Financial windfalls, election victories, and discovering your opponent’s plans are all potential Advantages. Advantages help to model consequences that aren’t easily described as Complications for your opponent.

These effects are the core of the rules system. When you’re in a conflict, both characters will end up taking a Complication or gaining an Advantage. If you spend Twists, you will typically create an Advantage for yourself or a Complication for someone else. When you need more Twists, you gain them by taking a Complication. Almost everything in the game ties into the Effect system.

Advantages and Complications are broken up into five levels, as shown below. The number shown is the Twist cost for that level. There are no even-leveled Effects – you can’t choose something between Moderate and Major, for example.

  • Trivial (1): Immediate and ignorable, no new info.
  • Minor (3): Short term, low impact, skeletal info.
  • Moderate (5): Long term, high impact, or accurate info.
  • Major (7): Long term, high impact, and accurate info.
  • Critical (9): Rule of Force.

Details for each level are below. Examples of these effects in play can be found in the sections on Conflict and Approach use. The Advice section has tips for GMs on how to handle Complications.

Trivial (1)

Immediate and minor impact to unnamed characters. No important information is involved. Such Complications are used to provide flavor and verisimilitude rather than game effects. Examples: Your foe is annoyed or distracted. Your opponent is barely taxed. Observers lose track of a single being among dozens during a riot. Prevent yourself from stumbling at an unimportant time. Give your foe a moment of doubt.

Players may only willingly accept one Trivial Complication per game session.

Minor (3)

Minor effects are short-term, low-impact, and involve skeletal information. They can still be potent if several build up, but they are unlikely to be a problem in the short term. Examples: An escape, but not without consequence. A delay or acceleration in plans. Your location is revealed to a distant enemy or vice versa. You are pointed toward the next piece of the puzzle. Minor but noticeable monetary impact. Minor injury.

Moderate (5)

Moderate effects are long-term, or high-impact, or involve accurate information, but not more than one. Examples: Kidnappings. Your actions are revealed to a distant enemy or vice versa. Unable to access a Nature or Profession. Core Values reduced or increased. You are befriended by the enemy. Plans significantly delayed or accelerated. Material possessions destroyed. Substantial injury. Resources severely taxed or substantially increased. A clean escape.

Major (7)

Major effects are long-term and high-impact and can involve accurate information. Examples: Deluded by the enemy, or able to successfully feed them misinformation. Unable to access several Natures or Professions. One Core Value or Core Expression changed. Convinced of a particular fact or falsehood. Important secrets revealed to the enemy. Allies turn against you. Friends lost. Your Society rejects you. Severe injury. Unconsciousness. Resources lost forever.

Critical (9)

Effects at this level may bring the Rule of Force into play. Examples: Death and fates worse than death. All Core Values changed. A major character is dying or near death. Your target is unable to exercise any number of Natures or Professions. You are exiled from your beloved homeland. Extensive brainwashing. Friends’ allegiances reversed. Long-term plans ruined, or coming to fruition immediately.

Players may only willingly accept one Critical Complication per game session.

The Big Deal Rule

A Complication that does not cause problems for its character is not a Complication. Twists are not gained from Complications whose effects can be easily ignored.

Recovery

It is possible to recover from any Complication, short of certain items at the Critical level. Complications created through conflict can be removed by the use of Approaches or magical healing, but those willingly accepted in order to gain Twists cannot (see the Frying Pan Rule).

The general length of time that Complications last is “until you get them fixed,” which can vary greatly depending on the situation the characters find themselves in. Seeing a psychologist, visiting a hospital, spending time to reconnect with a friend, all of these can be useful actions to take if you need to recuperate. See also the Healing art.

For some Complications (such as “befriended by the enemy”), the character may not be capable of fixing their own issues, and may need help from others. Complications left unaddressed may persist indefinitely, but it is recommended that only Major Complications have impact beyond a single game session, and only Critical ones should have permanent effects.

Large-Scale Complications

Large and well-protected groups with the Infrastructure descriptor can also suffer from Complications. However, because of their size, a slightly different set of Complications is used. In general, affecting Infrastructure-sized groups is easier with Plots or Projects.

Basic Actions

Characters taking unopposed actions will find success or failure based primarily on four factors: their Core Values, their Expertise, their Nature scores, and their players’ ingenuity. For each factor, determine a “yes” or “no” answer based on the following guidelines.

  • Core Values: Does the character care about the outcome of the event? Are they willing to put in the time and effort required?
  • Expertise: Does the character have a Profession that covers these sorts of tasks? Do they have substantial experience in this area, as represented by a properly-ranked Profession?
  • Nature: Are the character’s Nature scores up for the job? Are they enhanced in such a way as to make the task reasonable? Does the character have tools or know arts that would help in this area?
  • Ingenuity: Is the task one that is simple, easy, or obvious given the character’s attributes? If not, can the player invent a way in which to use those attributes and solve the problem? Can the player redefine the problem to be a solvable one, or find a way around the issue?

This approach to solving a problem is known as answering the Four Questions, or using a Basic Action. Conflicts between characters (whether PCs or NPCs) should not be handled using these rules; see the sidebars about “Conflict vs. Contest” and “Serious Threats are Sentient”.

If you find the answers to be “yes” in three out of four categories, you should consider the task accomplished. The higher the character’s attributes, the faster the task can be done. If all four categories are on the character’s side, the task will be completed in style, quickly, and with a minimum of fuss.

If two or more of the answers are “no,” however, the character is doomed to failure. Without enhanced Natures, the character may know how to achieve something without actually being able to accomplish it – the classic “If only I had a...” Lack of Expertise can lead to botched efforts and imperfect work that falls apart quickly. Lack of an appropriate CV leads to a an uninspired effort, short on passion or dedication. A lack of ingenuity on the player’s part cements these, and ingenuity on its own can only accomplish so much.

Restrictions

When considering particularly complex or skill-oriented tasks, Expertise is the most important factor. Certain tasks simply cannot be attempted, let alone completed, without the appropriate skills in place. Someone who has never picked a lock before is doomed to stand aimlessly before it, stymied by a lack of knowledge. The shark-filled waters of nation-state politics are likewise impossible to navigate without some experience. The GM should be fairly strict on this front. The level of Profession required should rarely be more than 2, but one must have the right skill for the job.

Conversely, the applicability of Core Values should be fairly wide when it comes to basic actions. A character who has the skills and technology necessary to complete an action can succeed if they care about something broadly related to the goal. The Self-Preservation CV will be relevant to many critical goals.

Positive Modifiers

There are three ways to improve your odds of success in a Basic Action:

Competitive Advantage

Some characters have a “Competitive Advantage” in a particular Nature or Profession. Competitive advantage can take the answer to one of the Four Questions from a “no” to a “maybe”, or from “maybe” to “yes”. This can be especially useful when the character needs a Nature that he or she does not have. It enables the character to rely on unusual tricks and cutting-edge techniques from a different Nature to achieve unexpected and amazing results.

Competitive advantage has other benefits as well: It gives +1 before multiplication in Conflicts, and see also “Lateral Strategies”.

Pushing Hard

When you are on the border between success and failure, having a little extra effort to put in can make the difference. You may spend one (and only one) point of Reserve to take the answer to one of the Four Questions from a “no” to a “maybe”, or from “maybe” to “yes”.

Assistance from Others

“Two heads are better than one” is a common saying for a reason. Team efforts, whether from just one assistant or a large group, can take the answer to one of the Four Questions from a “no” to a “maybe”, or from “maybe” to “yes”.

Assistance is most often helpful in applications of raw Nature, such as strength or peer pressure. Some Professions benefit from multiple people working in parallel; others suffer from a “too many cooks” effect. The GM should make their best call here. Teamwork’s most reliable impact is that it simply speeds things up. Ten people may not quite work ten times as fast as one person, but they can come close. See the Ritual rules for more detail.

Teamwork in conflicts works differently.

Mishaps

Because failure can create interesting situations, and even the most powerful can fail sometimes, there are two ways your character might fail at a Basic Action even if it seems they should succeed.

These rules are referenced in many other places in this book, for good reason: they're the best ways to pick up Twists during game play.

The Bad Luck Rule

The GM may offer you some Twists to turn one of your answers to one of the Four Questions from a “yes” to a “no”. This is intended to be used to model those times when you should be able to handle the situation, but dumb luck intervenes, regardless of your magical might. You call your patron by the wrong name. You can’t find your keys. You tap a flow with the wrong aspect. Life gives you lemons, but you get some Twists.

The number of Twists you receive is dependent on the level of Complication that is being offered. The total is reduced by your Power score, but you can still get one Twist even if your Power score is higher than the Complication level.

You are not required to accept the offered Twists. You may reject them and succeed despite the bad luck. If you do accept the Twists, treat this as a voluntary Complication – the Frying Pan Rule applies.

Players can also trigger the Bad Luck Rule at any time just to get Twists. See the Approaches section for more on selecting Complications for your character.

The Worst Enemy Rule

The Bad Luck Rule (above) is for times when you fail despite your enhancements. Sometimes, though, your power is exactly the problem. You’ve created too much aspected flow to handle. You called up something you cannot put down. Your mind is stuck in the mindset for war-magic and you need to escape. You are your own worst enemy.

In such circumstances you won’t be able to get out of the problem using your Tradition and Professions alone. Therefore, you receive the full Twist amount, without any reduction for your Power.

Just like with the Bad Luck Rule, players can trigger this to cause problems for their character and gain Twists. However, only one Complication from the Worst Enemy Rule can be active at once. Your character can have as many Bad Luck Rule Complications active at once as you desire.

Do I need a Core value to Tie my Shoes?

No, you don’t need a relevant CV just to tie your shoes. Not every interaction needs to be mediated by a game mechanic. Basic actions are designed to answer whether characters can do things that are difficult or challenging. If something is clearly trivial, don’t use these rules for it, just let it happen. Most day-to-day tasks can be assumed to be successful.

What counts as “trivial” varies from person to person, of course. Even “day-to-day” actions can be quite impressive for those with high Nature scores. Demolishing a stone castle is trivial for a high-War character, as is designing one a for an architect with 500 years of experience. Demolishing a reinforced bunker quickly or designing a grand cathedral would still be considered challenges.

Lateral Strategies

If one wanted to tear down a door, the usual method would be to strike with overwhelming force. Hurl lightning or employ the Mountain-Shattering Fist, and the door is sure to fall.

Or one could turn into a giant sloth, twenty feet tall with huge claws. Giant sloths sound really great at wrecking stuff.

Is that War or Self? Which should your character use?

The answer is normally War. War is the Nature that deals with devastation; it’s the clear answer. Self is about expression and the soul. If you want to wreck something, you’d normally use War. However, there is clearly an argument that it should at least be possible to tear down a door using Self.

In order to use the “wrong” Nature for an action, you need to have a Competitive Advantage in that Nature or in a Profession related to what you’re doing. In story terms, your culture has studied and relied on that technology so extensively that they’ve discovered unusual applications for it. You can then substitute that Nature for others with a -1 penalty.

Some substitutions make more sense tan others. Substituting Mystery for Trickery typically makes a fair amount of sense. Substituting War for Communion makes less sense, but might still be possible under fairly specific conditions, such as sending messages across a distance or healing post-traumatic stress.

Examples of Basic Actions

Onyx Grace is trying to translate an ancient historical text on the nature of ghosts. They are trained as a Linguist, have a CV of “Knowledge for the sake of lost souls”, and have a reasonable Mystery score. That hits three out of the Four Questions, so eventually they’ll succeed.

A Returner is irritated by a sizable stone in his field. It needs to be moved if the plow is to get through. His CV of “My Farm” is clearly applicable in this case. Without magical assistance he cannot simply pick it up and move it. However, the player suggests that, as an experienced farmer, his character can probably hook up chains to an ox and pull the stone out. The GM thinks that’s a good enough solution, so that ticks the “ingenuity” box and the stone gets moved.

Mien of Honor is buried beneath a ton of rubble. The Self-Preservation CV certainly applies here, but they do not have enough War or Industry to move the materials, nor any Professions that might apply. No amount of ingenuity is going to help. They’re stuck beneath the stone, and should probably consider using Twists to get out of this.

Drysea Noontime is creating a piece of artwork suitable for exhibit. The GM requires Artist 2 and Communion 3 for this particular audience. She has Artist 3 and Communion 4, so there’s no problem there. She also has a CV entitled “Immortality Through Art,” which definitely applies. The player isn’t feeling very inventive, and can’t think up a description of the piece, but there’s no need to – three of the Four Questions are a “yes,” so Drysea creates a beautiful work of art.

Linker of Rings should be able to navigate the local legal system easily. She has Mystery 3, Lawyer 3, and “Loopholes are my friends” as a CV. The GM would prefer to throw some plot in the way, and offers her player a few Twists via the Bad Luck Rule. Linker’s player thinks about it for a few seconds and takes the Twists. Linker gets her legal terms confused.

Star-Seeker is attempting to convince a group of Sovereigns that they wish to purchase many lakh-thaums of flow from him and not from another. The GM rules that because this is an opposed action, it is a conflict, and the Four Questions do not apply. They turn to the conflict rules.

Last Gleam of the Cerulean Skin is weathering an ice storm. He didn’t come to the Basin prepared for this. The Self-Preservation CV comes into play, but he has Industry 1 and no rating in Explorer. With no way to check off more than two of the four Questions, Gleam’s player starts looking at the Twist rules instead.

Conformity of the High Canals wants to move a ley line. He has Industry 4 and Theurge 3, but no relevant Core Value. His player suggests hiring some local assistants who might bring more enthusiasm to the job. The GM rules that this will also need a Ritual, since it’s a fairly large undertaking, so those assistants will come in handy in more ways than one.

Delight, who Stormed the Walls, is building a soul repository to trap one of her enemies. The GM rules that this Ritual requires Self 3 and Theurge 3. Delight has Self 3, and a CV entitled “Hunt them until the end”, but no Theurge skill. Her player suggests that Delight could change one of her own CVs to match one of her opponent’s and thus create a mystic connection between them through which the soul-trap could work. The GM points out that quickly changing one’s Core Values is a Self 5 task. Delight’s player considers spending the time to alter her CVs the slow way, but decides not to play through that sort of experience and gives up on the task. Delight will just have to trap her opponent a different way.

The Book that Breaks the Truth wants to create some fancy magical topiary that can come to life. The GM calls this Trickery 2, Farmer 2. Unfortunately, Book is a prolocutor, not a gardener. They have Trickery 5 (with Competitive Advantage!), and a “Catch them by surprise” CV, but no Farmer profession. They don’t even have Theurge (Earthpower) or Explorer to substitute for it, and Book’s player is out of ideas for how to do the magic. Book spends a point of Reserve to switch a question from “no” to “maybe”, and then uses their Competitive Advantage in Trickery to switch the “maybe” to a “yes”. That’s three “yes” scores, and some awful grubby-looking magical topiary is the result.

The Consequences of Failure

Because of the all-or-nothing nature of basic actions, the consequences of failure can sometimes seem severe, as in the example above with the person trapped under a ton of rubble. Remember that after a player’s character fails at a critical action, the next step is typically the use of Twists. If a Complication arises because of this, that’s exactly how the game is supposed to work.

Not all failures are life-ending. Some simply indicate that the character isn’t capable of solving the problem quickly enough or decisively enough. Sometimes you simply accept the failure and move on.

Failure on the Four Questions does not constitute a Complication and does not provide Twists unless the Bad Luck Rule or Worst Enemy Rule are in place.

Conflict

Basic actions and the Four Questions are designed to handle unopposed activities. However, the more exciting parts of the game happen when your character is facing opposition.

Conflict Summary

When characters come into conflict, follow these steps:

  1. Each side adds a Core Value and a Profession together, then multiplies by a Nature score to get a total.
  2. Declare goals and intended Complications or Advantages based on the difference in totals (see below).
  3. Describe the conflict, especially how the effects of the conflict occur.

Conflict Results

To assign Complications in a conflict, compare the two scores. The side with the higher score will end up with a better result, as shown below:

  • Within 10% of each other: Stalemate. Both sides suffer a Minor Complication.
  • Less than 50% difference: Narrow victory. The winner takes a Minor Complication, while the loser suffers a Moderate Complication.
  • 50% difference or more: Decisive victory. The winner takes a Minor Complication, while the loser suffers a Major Complication.
  • 2x or more: Crushing. The winner takes a Trivial Complication, and the loser suffers a Critical Complication.
  • 3x or more: No contest. The loser is at the winner’s mercy, taking a Critical Complication while the winner is unharmed.

Trading Complication for Advantage

Complications are flexible, but they describe only negative effects. If you are looking for a more positive effect, you can trade in your ability to inflict a Complication to get an Advantage for yourself. The level of Advantage you gain is the same as the level of the Complication you would have inflicted.

Just like Complications, Advantages need to make sense in terms of the conflict that creates them. For instance, if you are falsifying bank records for your own benefit, it makes sense that you could create an Advantage that describes your increased wealth. On the other hand, if you’re shooting someone from beyond the horizon, using your Advantage to gain secret information probably doesn’t make much sense.

Conflict in Detail

Conflict occurs when one character’s goals are incompatible with another’s and they are unable or unwilling to come to a compromise. When it becomes clear that this is the case, every side involved should state their goal explicitly. This is a player-level statement – the characters may not realize each other’s true goals, or even that they are in a conflict at first. (See Subtle Conflict for more about how to handle this.) However, the stakes should always be clear to the players.

The consequences of a conflict come in the form of Complications and Advantages. Unlike Complications accepted to fuel Approaches, these Complications do not provide Twists, nor do the Advantages cost Twists.

Each side should have a particular effect in mind going into the conflict, one that can represent the character’s actions and intentions accurately in game or story terms. Only one item from a particular level should be chosen. If you inflict a Complication on your foe, you cannot choose more than one effect. You also cannot buy multiple weaker Complications instead of a more serious one – for instance, you can’t trade in a Major (5) for a Minor (3) and two Trivial (1) Complications. However, you can choose to show mercy and inflict a single lesser Complication if you desire.

While we will refer to Complications as the typical outcome of a conflict for ease of writing, it is typically possible to gain an Advantage rather than inflicting a Complication if you prefer.

Along with your goal, you should state which CV, Nature, and Profession your character will use to achieve it. If you lack a suitable Profession, you cannot achieve that goal.

Once you have your goal and its means stated, add the levels of the relevant Core Value and Profession, then multiply that by your Nature score to get a total. You should end up with a score between 0 and about 50. You will be comparing your total to that of your opponent using the conflict table.

For example, if your total is 36, and your opponent’s total is 20, you have scored more than 50% higher than your opponent, but less than double. This puts you in the “Less than x2” level. You will take a minor Complication, but you will inflict a Major Complication on your opponent.

The character with the higher score rarely comes out unscathed – both sides in a conflict usually take a Complication. It might even be that the side with the higher score doesn’t “win” in the way they would like, but that they exact a worse punishment on the lower-scoring side.

It is vitally important to note that characters with Mystery 3+ can read Nature scores, Professions, CVs, and Traditions without effort, and characters with Communion 3+ can do the same for Core Values. As a result, characters are likely to know their fate before engaging in a conflict. The phrase “I don’t think I can do much here” should be in every character’s vocabulary, followed immediately by “Let’s see where I can be more effective.”

Professions in Conflicts

It is assumed that you will be switching tactics and pulling tricks during a conflict. You might use an unexpected Nature, such as intimidating people using Trickery (rather than War) to creep them out, or creating a complex contract using Industry (instead of Mystery). However, there is only one Profession involved. Even if you set someone up with a few verbal quips, a punch to the gut is still thrown with Soldier, or perhaps Athlete (Boxer). Your final goal is what determines the Profession involved, not the means that you use to achieve that goal. You can’t punch someone with Athlete (Boxer) and make them lose money.

Someone with a very high level in a Profession is likely to be able to use it for more purposes, such as using Magistrate to work with a media outlet and release a missing-persons story. Overlap is inevitable and ok. Try to keep this to level 3-5 of a Profession; be more strict with characters at the 1 or 2 level.

Conflicts Don’t Make Twists

Because it’s worth repeating: Complications resulting from conflicts do not get you Twists. The second you get into a conflict, you are bound by the Complication that results, and you get no reward for that (beyond whatever Complication you may inflict on your opponent).

Adjusting Conflicts

Any abilities or technologies that give a “bonus” in conflicts give +1 to a character’s effective Profession + CV score, before it is multiplied by Nature.

The GM may make simple adjustments to either side in a conflict by adding or subtracting up to two effective levels of Profession. For instance, a character trying to escape pursuit across a barren plain might suffer -1 to the Spy profession for lack of a place to hide, while a character using Locality to hide in their hometown might receive an effective +2.

Characters suffering under the effects of a Complication might also be penalized in appropriate ways. For instance, one whose faith has been shaken might act with a lower Core Value, and one who is injured would have certain Natures reduced.

Competitive Advantage

Competitive Advantage gives a +1 in conflicts before multiplication.

Teamwork in Conflicts

When working as a team, use the following table to determine the effectiveness of the group. Apply this bonus to the highest-score character before multiplying.

Working alone: +0
Two people: +1
3 – 10 people: +2
11 – 30 people: +3
31 – 100 people: +4
101 – 300 people: +5
x3 bigger = +1 more

Relative bonuses generally cap out at +4. For massively parallel processes (e.g. economics), cap at +6 instead.

If your target is large enough and structured enough, it may have one or more levels of the Infrastructure descriptor.

Conflict Spot Rules

“Spot rules” are rules for specific situations that don't easily fall into other categories. Conflict has a lot of these.

The Obligatory Instant Death Cutscene rule

While most characters in this game are immortal, that doesn’t mean they’re invincible. There are many, many things in this game that can instantly incapacitate your character without warning. Characters might be buried under a mountainside, thrown into the Red Space, paralyzed by a never-ending dream, turned into a frog, or chained to a rock and hurled off the edge of the world.

Whenever the GM is about to deploy something that would remove a PC from play, they are obligated and required to describe a short scene detailing the hazard. The characters have Approaches, after all, and can often find ways to avoid such fates. The players should not “metagame” a way out of this, using knowledge their characters could not have, but are highly encouraged to use Twists to good effect.

The OIDCR does not generate Twists on its own. See Sources of Twists for things that do, and ways for tapped-out players to get a few Twists in this kind of situation.

Large-Scale Conflicts

Conflicts between groups large and organized enough to qualify for the Infrastructure descriptor use the Large-Scale Complications and other Infrastructure rules.

Attacking Infrastructure

Some groups and objects in Sorc have the Infrastructure descriptor. From individual city-states at Infrastructure I to entire civilizations at Infrastructure III, they are substantially more durable and effective in conflicts. When a small group or single person is in a Conflict against something with Infrastructure, use these rules.

First, the larger group applies the maximum Teamwork bonus (see the Teamwork in Conflicts rules) for the type of conflict involved: +4 for most conflicts, +6 for highly parallel ones.

Second, if the larger group would suffer a Complication, the level of that Complication is reduced by one step for each level of Infrastructure (e.g. from Moderate to Minor). If the smaller group also has Infrastructure, use the difference in levels to find the reduction. This reduction also applies to any Advantage the smaller party might try to gain. Finally, when applying Complications to a target with Infrastructure, use the Large-Scale Effects list. The normal set of Effects is designed for human-level conflicts, and the Effect chart in that section is a better choice for such large-scale effects.

Multiple Opponents

When three or more people are in a single conflict, first make sure that their goals do indeed conflict with each other. You may be able to simplify this down to several one-on-one conflicts. If several people team up to take down a larger threat, and then squabble amongst themselves, treat it as two regular conflicts, one after another. Use the teamwork rules.

In the case of a true free-for-all, with many conflicting goals and each participant (or group) out for themselves, things can easily ratchet up in intensity. You are allowed to inflict Complications on each and every participant you face, based on the difference in your scores. You do not have to inflict Complications on everyone if there are people you would rather leave out of the affair, but it is your option to inflict one if your score allows it.

Multiple opponents cannot escalate separately against a single target so as to leave one person un-escalated. Escalations affect the entire conflict.

Conflicting Goals

You’ve found the spy in your ranks, and are chasing him down. You have only a narrow advantage – if you escalate, you can exact a Major Complication, but the spy gets a Moderate Complication in return. The spy chooses “your foe escapes unharmed” to represent his escape; you choose “kidnapped” to represent the capture. What happens?

What happens is that you both must choose new goals. The original question of the chase may remain unanswered, or may be obvious given the situation, but this system rarely allows for a pure “zero-sum” conflict where one side is the untouched winner and the other loses completely.

You might choose “Important Secrets Revealed”, while the spy might choose “Befriended by the Enemy”. You get the information you want from the spy, but in the process, he earns your trust. Who wouldn’t let their friend go in such a situation?

Escape!

Characters who have been caught and are in a conflict they want no part of may simply wish to escape. You may gain a Minor (3) Advantage to state that you have escaped, but that you suffer the effects of your opponent’s assault. If you can get to the Moderate (5) level, you can declare that you escape unharmed, and your foe cannot target you with a Complication, though she may still gain an Advantage.

Subtle Conflicts

Particularly masterful characters may be able to defeat others without having them even realize they have been bested. Reduce your multiplier from Nature by 1 to completely hide your actions from your opponent, winning secretly. Your reduced Nature cannot exceed your regular Trickery score for this purpose. You may still end up suffering a Complication due to exhaustion or use of resources.

In certain low-tech situations, where some characters have Natures rated below 2, it is easier for high-powered characters to enter into a subtle conflict. For instance, a Returner would be unlikely to recognize the effects of some of the more subtle magical curses, and may think that someone is just acting like a jerk. They may not realize that some characters are protected by divine providence, and might just assume they were lucky – if the effect isn’t too blatant. In such cases the GM may reduce or waive the usual penalty for a subtle conflict.

Escalation

The conflict rules do not assume a knock-down, drag-out, anything-goes environment. However, as some people say, “There’s no kill like overkill.”

Any character with a Core Value rated at 3 or higher can spend a point of Reserve to escalate a conflict. Both sides then receive a Complication one level higher than would otherwise be indicated. Conflicts can only be escalated twice: once by each side.

Characters can only escalate when their Core Value aligns directly with the conflict in question. Characters with CVs aligned with peace, non-violence, and similar ideas can also spend Reserve to de‑escalate a conflict, mitigating Complications instead of worsening them.

Instant Kill, Not Instant Fight

Almost all weapons and techniques in this game are capable of doing what they need to in a single stroke. An unprotected and unprepared person, or one caught by surprise, will fall almost instantaneously before the forces brought to bear in high-powered combat. Fights are handled through the Conflict system because characters will either have the Natures to detect weapons brought to bear, the Expertise to have the hairs stand up on the back of their necks, or enough Twists to handle things via the Obligatory Instant Death Cutscene Rule.

Twists Uber Alles

Remember that Approaches and Twists automatically trump all other measures, regardless of the relative difference in other scores. A character with a reasonable supply of Twists can easily escape unharmed against massively superior opposition. Meanwhile, one with a pending Complication will face it, regardless of what countermeasures are employed.

No Applicable Spells?

If you’re involved in a conflict where none of the Universal Expressions apply (which is rare), and you have no applicable Core Expressions, and you can’t or won’t spend Reserve to cast a non-Core Expression, treat your relevant Nature score as 1.

Wands and Weapons

What happens when a Returner with War 1 picks up a hellbow rated at War 4? The Returner now has War 4, limited to just those purposes for which the bow can be used – most likely, shooting people and blowing things up.

Note that those purposes don’t include defense, or keeping hold of the bow if someone attempts to disarm the Returner. For the purposes of inflicting Complications in a Conflict, the Returner uses the x4 multiplier for War 4. However, for the purposes of avoiding Complications, the multiplier drops to the Returner’s native x1. You’re effectively running two conflicts at once, one offensive and one defensive This can lead to a little more bookkeeping, but it gives a fairly sensible result.

Conflict Advice

Serious Threats are Sentient

The conflict system should be used for dealing with intelligent opponents. If a character is up against a non-sentient force, even a complex ritual, use the Four Questions to resolve the event. If you’re uncertain of where to draw the line, the relevant question is this: Does the obstacle in your character’s way have Core Values? If not, it’s not sentient and should be addressed with the Four Questions.

Contest vs. Conflict

Some head-to-head interactions are not conflicts in the way this system handles them. Contests of strength or speed, comparisons of pure skill, and situations where no one stands to truly lose anything might be contests, but they’re not Conflicts. For such purposes you can simply compare Nature or Profession scores, using other scores or Competitive Advantage to break ties. Conflicts are situations where there is some interplay between the people involved. They are situations where power, skill, and willpower are all necessary. Most importantly, they are situations where losing is meaningful, and many outcomes are possible. If that isn’t true in your situation, don’t use the conflict rules to handle it.

Tactics vs. Strategy

In most games, you as a player are responsible for your characters’ tactics during a conflict. Your choice of whether to go for the high ground or hide behind a barrier, which opponent to flank and which to attack directly, etc. can make a big difference in how a conflict plays out and who ends up as the eventual winner.

This game doesn’t have that. Your character is the one responsible for tactical decisions. You can choose to expand that and play through those choices, or you can just hide that step behind the conflict resolution mechanics. Either way, you as a player don’t need to worry about whether your choices are right for your character tactically. Your character takes care of that on their own.

Instead, you are in charge of your character’s strategic decisions. You choose which conflicts to take part in and how. You decide what sort of Complications to accept or inflict, and what Advantages to gain. You handle the long-term decisions rather than the blow-by-blow decisions.

Sorcerously Advanced is a game where you typically know whether you can win a conflict before it starts. The question is not “Will the dice land in our favor?” but instead, “What can I do with my loss to ensure my team’s victory?”

Timescales

Your chosen Effects should be achievable on the same timescale as your opponent’s. If someone assaults you with an axe and an intent to do grievous bodily harm, responding with a carefully orchestrated whisper campaign is not going to stop you from ending up in multiple pieces. If Mystery is your thing, perhaps predicting their movements or Spirit-Walking your way out will help you come out with only minor wounds. If your opponent in a long-term action comes back with a shorter-term goal, you can feel free to revise yours in order to match theirs. You can return to your previous efforts afterward, pending the results of the conflict.

Switching a conflict to a shorter timescale can sometimes be very useful. It forces your opponent into a faster conflict, presumably one where you have the advantage. However, winning the short conflict will not give you the same results as winning the longer one. If you’re sick of a political debate, you can interrupt it with a fistfight, but it might not get you the results you’re looking for.

Example Conflicts

There has been a strange discovery near a Nova Commonwealth city, and the Archive wants to be first to examine it. Since the Archive genuinely believes it might be dangerous, their representative tries to impress that upon the Nova representative. For their part, Nova has several people who would love to investigate first, so they try to convey the primacy of their claim.

Archivist: Communion 3, Prolocutor 3, “Knowledge for a Better Future” core value rated at 3. Total score: 3 x (3+3) = 18.

Nova representative: Communion 4, Politician 2, “Keep our Researchers Happy” core value at 3. Total score: 4 x (2+3) = 20.

Nova’s advantage is just barely above 10%. They take a minor complication – the Archive is likely to bring this up as leverage in future negotiations, but they get nothing today. The Archivist takes a Moderate Complication: either long-lasting, having major impact, or yielding substantial information. The representative picks the latter: they give the Archivists permission to investigate, but everything they learn will have to pass through the Commonwealth’s researchers before they send it back to the Archive.

Note that this is not a “take it or leave it” offer; this is what the Archivist agrees to when they leave the negotiating table. Both sides consider it a binding deal.

Sunflower Irony-Master, a necromancer of the Subtle Path, sneaks into the lair of the sleeping dragon Thyophrax, ready to battle it for its hoard, which contains a lost artifact. This may not have been the best idea.

Sunflower: War 2, Soldier 2, “Unmitigated Greed” 4. Total score: 2 x (2+4) = 12.

Thyophrax: War 5, Battle 4, “Hunger for Gems” 3. Total score: 5 x (4+3) = 35.

“Uh... that’s a lot” says Sunflower’s player. “Can I use Trickery instead? I’m way better at Trickery.”

“Well, Sunflower has enough Mystery that they’d know Thyophrax’s scores. Do you want to step back and not have declared battle?” asks the GM.

“I get to take it back?” asks the player.

“Sure. Just ask me what the scores are beforehand next time.”

“Ok. How’s its Mystery score?”

“Normally 4, but the mighty Thyophrax was asleep when you snuck in, so let’s call it 2.”

“Yeah, let’s try sneaking instead.”

Sunflower, a necromancer of the Subtle Path, attempts to sneak past the dragon Thyophrax and steal a single treasure from its hoard.

Sunflower: Trickery 5, Spy 3, “Unmitigated Greed” 4. Total score: 5 x (3+4) = 35.

Thyophrax: Mystery 2, Spy-eater 2, “Hunger for Gems” 3. Total score: 2 x (2+3) = 10.

Sunflower’s score is in the “3x or more” category. They take the treasure and leave, with the dragon none the wiser.

A Returner sailor comes back from time spent in the dangerous outside. Two young toughs try to rough her up for her money.

Sailor: War 1, Soldier 2, “Pride and Valor” core value rated at 3. Total score: 1 x (2+3) = 5.

Toughs: War 1, Soldier 1, “I deserve things” core value at 1, +1 Teamwork. Total score: 1 x (1+1+1) = 3.

The sailor is between 50% more and x2 the toughs’ total score, so this falls into “less than x2” category. The sailor takes a minor complication, while the toughs take a major one. The sailor’s player chooses to inflict some major injuries on the toughs, effectively depriving them of War and Soldier until they heal up. The toughs’ player decides that escaping is a pretty good idea here, so they use a minor advantage to escape (but not without harm).

Caller of Councils, a Nova administrator, is negotiating with Flow-Keeper, a merchant from Those Above. Caller is trying to get a good deal for the diversion of a massive ley line.

Flow-Keeper: Trickery 4, Merchant 3, “Bring Home The Bacon” 3. Total score: 4 x (3+3) = 24.

Caller: Mystery 3, Merchant 3, “Watch the Bottom Line” 4. Total score: 3 x (3 + 4) = 21.

Flow-keeper has a better than 10% advantage, but not by much, so this falls into the “less than 50%” category. Flow-keeper’s player chooses a Moderate Advantage: a small but long-term monetary gain. Caller’s player chooses to inflict a Minor Complication. Minor items have to be something that isn’t a big deal right now but could add up in the long term. Caller’s player says that Flow-keeper’s haggling is trying the patience of Nova Commonwealth, and if they do it too much things will get difficult for them.

Many Archive missions revolve around an archaeological dig. The dig itself doesn’t have an “opponent”, per se, or if it does they’re long dead. Only thinking beings count as opponents and trigger the conflict rules. For a dig, use the Basic Actions or Rituals rules.

Thin Line of Golden Dust, a Glimmermere artist, chases after a thief that stole their treasured magical pigments. This conflict could take many forms:

If Line is running, relying on their own personal speed and agility, they would probably use War.

If Line is flying by means of levitation or a magical object, they would use Industry.

On the other hand, if Line wants to shapeshift into an appropriate form (bird, fish, cheetah, adding wings to themselves, etc.), they would use Self.

Regardless of the Nature used, the right Profession is almost certainly Athlete.

One Hand’s Grace, a Diadem noble, is trying to bluff their way through a negotiation, intimating that they already have the upper hand in the search for an artifact and their opponent might as well give up. The Sight that Turns the Leaf, a Worldbuilder treasure hunter on the other side, remain suspicious.

Grace: Trickery 4, Merchant 4, “For My House” 3. Total score: 4 x (4+3) = 28.

Sight: Mystery 3, Magistrate 3, “Dig Deep” 3. Total score: 3 x (3+3) = 18.

This puts Grace just barely into the “Less than 2x” category. This nets them a major effect to use against Sight: something both long-term and powerful. Grace’s player decides that Sight leaves while Grace gets the artifact. Sight’s player gets a minor effect, and they use it to start building a case against Grace, painting them as untrustworthy.

Unwavering Eye, an Ægis shieldbearer, stands on watch, while The Potentate of the End, a Sovereign troublemaker, commands their demons to sneak them through the area unseen.

Unwavering Eye: Mystery 4, Magistrate 2, “Hold the line” 3. Total score: 4 x (2+3) = 20.

Potentate: Mystery 4, Spy 4, “Transgression” 3. Total score: 4 x (4+3) = 28.

This falls into the “less than 50%” category. The Potentate declares “escape without injury” as their Moderate Advantage, while Unwavering Eye deals a Minor Complication: it took more time than Potentate wanted. If they’re delayed again things could get dicey.

Note that while The Potentate undoubtedly knows arts like the Shroud and Spirit-walking, and Unwavering Eye is talented with the Sixth Sense, these fold into the background for many conflicts. They explain how the characters are competing, rather than letting them skip the contest.

Verity, who Built the Stair, an Unroyal citizen, confronts Scale of Tower Twelve, a Geometer, about their civilization’s alliance with Diadem, explaining Diadem’s many crimes. Scale doesn’t want to hear it – they like the Diadem, though they don’t have any specific CV related to them.

Verity: Communion 3, Prolocutor 3, “Let No One Reign” 4. Total score: 3 x (3+4) = 21.

Scale: Self 3, Lawyer 2, no applicable CV. Total score: 3 x (2+0) = 6.

This is an absolutely crushing victory, with Verity in the “x3 or more” category. A Critical Complication will let Verity give Scale a Core Value against Diadem, bringing them into the cause.

If Verity’s player had been a bit more greedy – for instance, trying to turn Scale into a rabidly violent anti-Diadem terrorist – the Self-Control CV would have come into effect, and the conversation wouldn’t have gone nearly as well. Sometimes it pays to tone back your Complications.

A group of Unroyal are fleeing from pursuing Diadem forces, using the Travel lattice. One of them, Liberty, holds the line against a half-dozen of Diadem’s warriors to buy her compatriots time. Note that the lattice is a -1 Null Zone, which leads to reduced Nature and Profession scores. These are already taken into account below.

Liberty: War 2, Soldier 2, “Let None Rule You” 4. Total score: 2 x (2+4) = 12.

Diadem warriors: War 3, Soldier 2, “Embrace Duty” 3. Teamwork modifier for six of them: +2. Total score: 3 x (2+3+2) = 21.

This puts Diadem’s warriors in the “less than x2” category. Normally, they would take a Minor Complication, while Liberty would take a major one.

Liberty’s player decides that’s not enough. They escalate, spending a point of Reserve, and also spend a Shift. Liberty will take a critical complication, but Diadem’s warriors will suffer a major one. Liberty’s allies make a clean escape as she moves like lightning and strikes like thunder.

Diadem’s player decides that for the Critical Complication, the warriors finally tear into Liberty, rendering her unconscious and throwing her over the edge, setting her drifting through the Travel lattice.

One of the PCs wants to show off their athletic talent with a newly-encountered Unruly Land, challenging them to their own game.

If the outcome is not important to the plot, just pick a winner based on Nature and Profession and move on with it. Don’t use Complications and Advantages for low-stakes events.

If the athlete’s goal is to impress the citizens of this new land, those are high enough stakes to use a Conflict. It might look like this:

Athlete: Communion 3, Athlete 2, “Don’t Hold Back” 3. Total score: 3 x (2+5) = 15.

Newcomers: Self 2, Athlete 3, “Go Team Go” 3. Total score: 2 x (3+3) = 12.

Note that the athlete is using Communion, not a more body-oriented Nature like Self or War. That’s because the goal here is to connect with others, not to win the game. If you win the game but look like a jerk, that defeats the purpose here.

An Archive researcher races against their foes in the Fellowship of Truth, each trying to decipher an ancient tablet before the other. This isn’t really a good use of the Conflict rules – there’s no back-and-forth here, no real interaction between the characters. Instead, treat it like a Ritual, tracking the time it takes to finish.

An Archive researcher races against their foes in the Fellowship of Truth, each trying to decipher an ancient tablet before the other. In addition, each side has dispatched agents to slow down the other side and (if possible) steal their results. This is a Conflict happening in the middle of a Ritual. Use the Ritual rules to see how long each side will take. If there’s enough time to sneak in and sabotage the other side, run the conflict. Typically a conflict like this would use Mystery or Trickery, with professions like Criminal or Spy.

A magus from the Fellowship of Truth tries to convince a Sovereign to turn against their fellows, arguing that betrayal is the sweetest transgression.

Magus: Trickery 4, Politician 2, “The Old ways” 3. Total score: 4 x (2+3) = 20.

Sovereign: Self 4, Prolocutor 2, “Transgression” -3, “Every Soul a Fortress” 4. Total score: 4 x (2–3+4) = 12.

Note that the Sovereign’s Transgression CV counts against their total in this case. They can still apply “Every Soul a Fortress” to resist.

The magus is in the “Less than x2” category, letting them dole out a Major Complication to the Sovereign – probably something like “Planning to turn on my friends at the right moment.” Of course, if someone finds out, they might be able to convince the Sovereign that turning around and betraying the person who convinced you to betray people was even better.

A smooth-tongued criminal is being held in prison by a Returner cult. They are due to be executed in the morning. The criminal has Trickery 5 and Politician 4, but this is a -1 Null Zone.

Criminal: Trickery 4, Politician 3, “Self-Preservation” 4. Total score: 4 x (3+4) = 28.

Returner: Self 1, Magistrate 1, “Self-Preservation” 4. Total score: 1 x (1+4) = 5.

“Bam. That’s a more than 5-times advantage. I want to talk him into handing me the keys and killing himself,” says the player.

“Well that’s disturbing,” says the GM. “Also, I’m looking at the Trickery-nature page and it feels like that’s out of scope. Trickery just doesn’t make you convincing enough fast enough to talk someone into killing themselves. Why don’t you just use Trickery to walk out of the cell?”

“Come on, I can get like double a Critical Complication on this guy!”

“I know, but not that one. Not in the amount of time you’ve got.”

“Ok, fine. How about I make him realize his career and belief system are a sham and he lets me out?”

“So kinda like the Complications where you rewrite someone’s CVs?”

“Yeah.”

“Ok, that sounds better. After an hour or two of conversation with you, the guard doubts everything he’s done for the past ten years. He goes home to rethink his life and leaves you with the keys.”

Magnesium Flare, an enraged Glimmermere war artist, strikes out with prismatic blasts of energy against the city that spurned their offers of artistic work.

The GM needs to decide how to assign the city’s statistics for this purpose. For Nature, she picks the average of the citizens’ Industry rating, since that’s what created the buildings. For Profession, she uses the average Samaritan rating of the city’s emergency response teams. Finally, she assigns those teams a generic “Protect Others” CV at 3.

Flare: War 4, Theurge (Glory) 3, “Famous or Infamous” 5. Total score: 4 x (3+5) = 32.

The City: Industry 3, Samaritan 2, “Protect Others” 3. Infrastructure I. Total score: 3 x (2+3+4) = 27.

Note the city’s extra +4 bonus from its Infrastructure rating.

Flare has an advantage between 10% and 50%. This delivers a Moderate complication while taking a Minor one. Flare, being quite upset, decides to escalate, spending a point of Reserve to increase the Complications to Major and Moderate.

The city’s Infrastructure advantage reduces the complication it takes to Moderate. Flare would like to escalate again, but any particular side only gets to do it once.

Flare would like to devastate buildings and vaporize bystanders. The city’s defenders would like to ensure that no one gets harmed. The GM decides that these two goals are probably in conflict with one another, and both sides must choose new Complications.

After some consideration, Flare’s player says that they instead threaten the city with certain devastation if they are scorned. A low-impact but long-term complication sows fear in the population. Flare’s player could reasonably ask for a temporary “Fear-driven” Core Value to work with when they talk with the city’s citizens in the future.

The city, in response to the threat, picks a short-term but high-impact complication: they capture and imprison Flare in expectation of a trial.

Don’t Overuse Conflict

You can use the conflict system for every single interpersonal event in the game. It’s not a good idea. Conflict is designed to handle situations where the outcome is vitally important. These are situations of serious competition, or where those involved feel a strong need to impose their will on others or stop said imposition. Normal conversation is not a conflict. Even strongly-worded arguments, even between high-Trickery individuals, are not necessarily conflicts.

Rituals

Characters often want to create a long-standing asset to solve a problem: a new cure for a necromantic disease, a curse with a peculiar effect, an army of automata, a ward against a newfound power, even a secret society. That’s what this system is for. Characters apply their intelligence and knowledge, as well as their myriad technological resources in the form of Reserve, to speed their ritual to completion.

This section lays out the rules for accomplishing ritual magic and gives some examples for what you can accomplish with it. What we don’t talk about here is what you can do quickly with magic, without a ritual. Some of that is described in the Natures section of the Character Creation chapter, but the Expression list in the Magic chapter describes what you can accomplish in much greater detail. Spells with the “Ritual” descriptor always require a ritual to cast.

To plan out your ritual, you should first determine the Nature score required for your ritual. The Nature itself (Mystery, War, etc.) is typically obvious. Sometimes the level will also be obvious, as for someone who wants to make a “cloak that hides things like Trickery 4”. Other times, the player and GM will need to talk and compare the ritual to existing works. Someone who wants to design a new kind of synergistic Rapport, for example, might set it as one level higher than existing Rapport techniques.

Next, determine the general type of the ritual:

Great Works: Projects that have long- lasting impact on society or fundamental metaphysics. Setting up a new colony, replacing a government, building a society, implementing a continent-sized transportation network, or discovering new principles of sorcery. Great works take a baseline of 50 years and can substantially change the game.

Major Rituals: Incremental improvements to existing technology, or rapid social change. Speeding up a magical process, creating a new Expression, curing a new and unknown disease, or changing corporate organization by implementing a known management method. Major projects take a baseline of 5 years and can alter large regions or groups.

Minor Rituals: Use of existing technology in a substantial manner, or making minor modifications to an existing technology. Developing a new curse or countercurse, creating a new building, running an advertising campaign, inoculating a village, or training an animal to complete a complex task. Minor projects take a baseline of 3 months and typically have local or minor impact.

Craft Rituals: Making something from a template or performing something well-practiced. Learning to play a song, assembling a set of furniture, knitting a scarf, performing weather-working or healing, or cleaning out the basement. Craft projects take 12 hours and typically have a small, immediate, tangible product.

These can change some based on the physical or metaphysical size of what’s being done. Weather-working is normally a Craft ritual, but someone who wanted to affect an island could use it as a Minor ritual, or conduct a Great Work to permanently affect an entire continent.

The times listed above assume that you have enough magical power available, either within yourself or through nearby infrastructure, and that you have the appropriate Profession rated at 2.

Some projects require more than one Nature. For example, designing something new will often be a Mystery task, while implementing that design will invoke other Natures. Model this by setting two separate Nature requirements for the project. Characters gain time steps (below) from the most advantageous Nature score that applies, not from both of them.

Once you have determined the project’s Nature score, you’ll be moving up or down the Time Ladder. Apply these guidelines:

  • Each increase in Profession or Nature moves you down the time ladder one step.
  • Access to outside resources (such as a Universal Repository) moves you down the time ladder one step.
  • The GM may rule some tasks to be particularly simple (down one step) or complex (up one step). Altering the skill level required is also reasonable for complex or simple rituals, but the required Profession rating should never exceed 3.
  • If your ritual requires Infrastructure to work (see “What do rituals require?”) and your current region is short one level, add 3 steps. If you are short 2 levels, you cannot attempt this ritual.
  • If your ritual would affect an entire group with an Infrastructure rating, increase the time. Infrastructure I adds 2 steps, II adds 4 steps, and III adds 6 steps. You may also need a successful Conflict to pull off this project. For rituals even more vast, see virtual infrastructure levels.
  • If you have people capable of helping, divide the time by [your Teamwork modifier plus one]. If you yourself have the Infrastructure descriptor, use the +4 Teamwork modifier that you usually receive – you divide times by 5.
  • If you are attempting a Ritual where you do not have a related Core Expression, invest an additional point of Reserve (see below).

You can conduct a single ritual at a time, or two with Mystery or Self at 3 or higher, or three with both rated 3 or higher (though you will eventually need to sleep). You cannot work “double shifts” on the same ritual to speed it up – some things just take time.

Not Whether, But When

Whether or not a ritual can be completed should be referred to the Four Questions. In most Sorc teams, the answer is almost inevitably “yes” – someone in the group will have the Nature and expertise needed to complete the project. The ritual system answers the more difficult question of how much time a project will take.

This is partially a metagame concern. Most groups are happy to take on rituals that can be completed within a day, but wary of longer-term endeavors that might bog down the game or be interrupted by outside events. Reserve helps the players choose which rituals are most worth their effort and focus on those.

Reserve Speeds Rituals

As mentioned in the Character Creation section, your character has a number of points of Reserve equal to their Power score. These points can be used to speed a ritual to completion.

If you want to complete your ritual faster, invest Reserve in it. Every point of Reserve you invest moves you down the ladder one step. In this way you can choose which actions you want to focus on and do those more quickly.

You cannot invest more points of Reserve in a ritual than you have points in an appropriate Core Value, or points in the appropriate Nature, whichever is lower. If you have no appropriate CV, you can still invest one point of Reserve per project.

Reserve that is invested in a ritual stays there until the completion of the ritual, whether because it creates its desired effect or because you had to interrupt it. In the meantime, your maximum Reserve is reduced by the amount that you keep invested.

When a ritual ends, you do not get the invested Reserve back immediately. You regain a point of Reserve every night that you get enough rest. (For characters with a high Self scores, this can be very little rest.) You regain an additional point while resting if you have a Core Value rated at 3 or higher that matches up with your current ritual. You cannot exceed your maximum Reserve.

What does Reserve do again?

To collect most of the rules about Reserve in one place:

  • You have a maximum number of points of Reserve equal to your current Power score. Even if you have a Power of 0, you will still have 1 point of Reserve available.
  • You regain a point of Reserve every night that you get enough rest. You regain an additional point if you have a Core Value rated 3+ that matches up with your current Ritual. You cannot exceed your maximum Reserve.
  • Spend a point of Reserve to switch Traditions. Adjust Twists and Reserve appropriately.
  • Spend a point of Reserve to use a magical art that’s neither Universal nor part of your Core Expressions, or to substitute one Profession for a similar one. You can’t do specialized actions.
  • In Basic Actions, one (and only one) point of Reserve can be spent to push your limits and change a “no” to a “maybe”, or a “maybe” to a “yes”.
  • In Conflicts, one (and only one) point of Reserve can be spent to escalate (or de-escalate) a conflict. Every side involved will take one level higher Complication than would normally be incurred (or lower, for de-escalation). You must also have an appropriate Core Value.
  • In Rituals, one or more points of Reserve (up to the level of an applicable CV) can be invested to buy steps on the time ladder. Each point of Reserve buys one step.

What do rituals require?

The term “ritual” may give the image of a single practitioner in a closed room, drawing symbols and cooking reagents until the project is done. For rituals that require Nature 2, that will do. Above that, one needs support.

First, all magical rituals require properly-aspected flow. If you're near an appropriate ley line, in a region with natural aspects, or if you and your assistants have Aspected Earthly Forms, those are all good ways to do it. Other times you'll need to work out a way to produce that aspect before you can start.

Rituals of Nature 3 require some minor resources or a number of assistants. A coven of witches, a tiny fork off a ley line, a set of troubleshooting guides stored in the Universal Repositories, or a well-enchanted relic will be sufficient. You’ll want to work in an area with Infrastructure I or better.

At Nature 4 one needs serious technological support. One could use most of the energy from a typical ley line, a set of high-end tools, or a dozen willing associates. You’ll need a region with Infrastructure II or better.

At Nature 5, rituals require most of the energy from a major ley line, a hundred eager cultists, access to a broad set of exceptionally pure reagents, or other such high-quality resources. It will be difficult to find this outside of an Infrastructure III region.

None of this applies if you’re merely applying a higher Nature to a lower-level ritual. You only need these things when accomplishing heavier-duty tasks.

Note also that the teamwork modifier only applies once you've passed the threshold for starting your ritual. If you're using a dozen casters to achieve a Nature 4 ritual, you'll need a dozen more to get the first level of teamwork modifier.

Rituals Make Prototypes

It is important to note that rituals create prototypes, not production-quality items. Expect quirks in automata. Expect side-effects in cures. Expect minor revolts in your empire.

Polishing out these imperfections takes Twist use or time. Treat this process as a new ritual requiring +3 time steps, with a maximum Reserve expenditure of 2. You can’t cut corners or throw extra power at this – you need careful testing and refinement.

Nature Score Benchmarks

If you’re not sure what Nature score a particular ritual should require, here are some guidelines:

  • Nature 2 covers basic mystical expressions of otherwise baseline human abilities. Something with Nature 2 is clearly magical, and potentially even world-changing, but it doesn’t do things that sheer ingenuity wouldn’t provide in the right circumstances. For example, someone skilled in exploration could find water in the wilderness, so a Industry 2 ritual could make an ever-full waterskin.
  • Nature 3 is where things become impossible to understand as anything but magic. It crosses boundaries: between worlds, into dreams, from emotional to physical. It touches things that are purely magical. Communion 3 could create a necklace that draws a sleeper into the Dream Realms.
  • Nature 4 acts upon the things from Nature 3. It’s a very “meta” level. Industry 3 can access a ley line, but Industry 4 can move it. Nature 4 can also make things from Nature 2 or 3 more convenient, such as when Trickery 4 gives you your own piece of Red Space.
  • Nature 5 gets big, fast, and serious. People from nonmagical cultures will probably be terrified by these things, from binding multiple souls to your body with Self 5 to creating a never-ending hall of mirrors with Trickery 5.

Example Rituals

Here are a few dozen things that one could do with ritual magic. As we’ve implied, this list is not intended to be exhaustive. It’s here to help you estimate the levels of your own rituals as you come up with new weird and amazing things magic can do in your setting.

Archaeological Dig: Retrieving a single artifact is Minor :: Mystery 2. Fully excavating the site of an ancient city would be Major.

Automata: See Mystic Servitors. Those rules are used regardless of what kind of servant you’re creating. If you’re making something with true free will, see Genesis below instead.

Automated Flow Detectors: Typically Minor :: Mystery 2. Many ritual devices emulate the sixth sense to trigger effects based on particular presences or events. The different aspects of flow reflect all of the vast, multifaceted world of the Basin, so these triggers can be quite fine-grained and discriminating. One mechanism might detect an intruder in a tomb by seeking out life-flow. Another could find soldiers in a crowd by seeking out War-essence, or romantic partners by detecting bonds of love.

Body Swaps: Taking minds from two different bodies and switching them with one another. Craft :: Self 4 + Communion 3. You need permission to enact this ritual; see Sovereignty.

Build a Secret Society: This is a Major ritual, requiring Nature 1 but Profession 3. If you believe in the cause, use Communion. Otherwise, use Trickery.

Cleaning Up Flow Pollution: This is difficult: you need to gather the flow and send it through a lengthy process of extracting or countering unwanted aspects, or pipe it away to a safe location. Major :: Industry 4 for a city-state-sized area, with +2 time shifts for an Infrastructure I target.

Communication Network: Minor for a spotty network that covers a city. Increase the ritual type to cover a continent, to improve coverage, or use Infrastructure levels to cover larger areas. Depending on how you’d like it to work, this could be done with Communion (if it’s mostly done via living relays), Industry (if it’s heliographs or other physical devices), or Mystery (if it’s more of a mutual surveillance network).

Enchanted Items: Sometimes you want to hand someone a flaming sword, or give your non-magical friend a crystal ball that will let them contact you. As a general guideline for creating enchanted items that aren’t on this list, a Craft ritual will create an item that allows someone to use a single, limited expression of an Art. Good examples include farspeech with only you, healing for their family, or the above-mentioned flaming sword. Minor rituals expand that to a wide range of applications: rapport with you, healing for anyone who produces a certain aspect of flow, a weather-controlling bowl. It takes a Major ritual to expand that to all the possible uses of a particular art, effectively allowing someone to use an Expression as if they knew it themselves.

Floating Architecture: For a single building or castle, if you have Industry 2, this is a Major ritual. If you have Industry 4, it’s Minor. Increase the type to Major or Great Work to put neighborhoods, cities, or islands aloft.

Genesis: Creating intelligent life is Major :: Communion 3 + Self 3. If they are the first of their kind, upgrade to Great Work. Plant or animal life is typically Minor :: Industry 3 instead. Raise the Nature level to create larger life forms, or to create life that is inherently magical on its own (like a dragon).

Group-Minds: Long-term use of Rapport can merge several minds into one as described in Sufficiently Advanced (SA2 page 16). If you’d like to do it faster and more deliberately, it’s Minor :: Communion 2. Raise the nature score to 3 if you want it to endure when bodies are removed from each other, or 4 if you want it to stretch across realms of existence.

Larger On The Inside: For something you can carry with you (the classic bag of holding), this would be Craft :: Trickery 4. For a house that Escher would envy, it changes to Minor :: Trickery 3 – larger spaces take more time, but stationary ones are easier to create.

Mass Entertainment: Want to put on a show on a large scale? You might use Earthpower to show epic stories in the clouds, which would require Craft :: Communion 3, with Profession 3 required. Using Glory to send holy dreams to particular sleepers across a city is much more personalized and direct, and would count as affecting a group with Infrastructure I, adding 2 time steps.

Move or Lock a Travel Lattice Point: Locking a latting point or towing it a few blocks is Craft :: Industry 4. Taking it farther away is liable to result in it simply snapping back to where it used to be.

New Dream Realm: After selecting the emotion or mental state exemplified by your dream realm, creating it is Minor :: Communion 3. The appropriate profession is Theurge.

Null Zones: Major :: Industry 1, requiring Theurge 3. This doesn’t even require any magic; just a willingness to literally move mountains. Add one time step to create a -2 Null Zone, or two time steps for a -3.

Oblation Automaton: Sometimes you have a literal ton of scrolls and clay tablets and you don’t want to spend the time to place them all into the Universal Repositories yourself. Building a device to commit them on its own while you do other things would be Minor :: Communion 3.

Rebuild your Tools: Some Paths require that their followers use particular tools in order to do magic. If yours are destroyed, but you have the appropriate materials to recreate them, you can accomplish that as Craft :: Self 2.

Redirecting Ley Lines: For an ordinary ley line, this is Minor :: Industry 4, with +2 time shifts for an Infrastructure I target. Moving a particularly large ley line or a nexus may count as Infrastructure II, or even III for the largest ones.

Replace a Core Expression: Minor :: Self 2. Any applicable Profession is ok.

Revamp a Grand Wonderwork: Maybe you’d like to improve the Universal Repositories’ searchability, or make the Red Space less horrific. Such changes are Major, requiring Nature 4 and Profession 3. They also require Infrastructure timeshifts, which can be substantial. Creating a Grand Wonderwork from scratch is, of course, a Great Work project.

Sawing Someone In Half: Presuming that you’re not taking the messy approach, you should enchant your beautiful assistant with Self 4 (for high-end shapeshifting), or potentially Self 2 if you’re the sort of goofball who would “lend someone a hand”.

Soul Repository: To catch a single soul is simple, a Craft :: Self 3 ritual using Clergy, Healer, Medium, or Theurge. If you’d like to provide a sort of afterlife for them, add Communion 4 or Trickery 4 to build a dream realm or hallucination. To build something that catches all the souls of the dead within a small valley would be Minor :: Self 3, but it would also be affecting a group with Infrastructure I, adding 2 time steps.

Vehicles: Sky yachts, auto-chariots, and other personal transportation would be minor rituals. Larger airships, sandworms, or whalesubs would be major rituals. Either would use Industry 2. If they'll be traveling through low-flow areas like space, use Industry 3. If they have weaponry, fold in War 2+ as well.

Vending Machines: At Industry 2, a craft ritual could construct a cornucopia that produces one thing a few times a day. A minor ritual could create a multi-purpose device, or one that continually produces a particular thing.

More Ritual Examples

The Magic section also has several examples of ritual arts. We’ve repeated a few of them below for reference:

Great Works: Clone an entire megalopolis (just the buildings). Create a group of mystical servitors large enough to qualify for Infrastructure I. Ward a group or area the size of Infrastructure I. Create the first of a new form of intelligent life.

Major Rituals: Giving the Gift. Warding a village or everyone in it. Create a new intelligent being. Build large airships. Clone a human being. Create thousands of mystical servitors.

Minor Rituals: Create a few dozen mystical servitors. Ward a large group or house. Clone a tree or animal. Switch your immortality from one Fount to another.

Craft Rituals: Baseline for enchantment, healing, mnemosurgery, and weatherworking. Ward a small group or a room. Conduct soul-work. Conjure large and complex items, or clone small and simple ones. Apply Distillation to a building. Create a single mystical servitor.

Approaches and Twists

While magic and Expertise are certainly powerful, they’re only half of the game. Twists alter the world around your character, bringing certain story elements into play. Twists are spent “through” Approaches to alter the story. The more Twists you spend, the greater the effect you can achieve. Whereas Natures and Professions have fairly narrow purviews and well-defined results, Approaches are broader and take a little more interpretation in their use.

This section looks at Approaches, Twists, and Complications in depth, to give you a better understanding of how they work and how you can exploit them to your advantage.

PCs Only

All characters in Sorc have Traditions, Civilizations, Societies, Shifts, Expertise, Nature scores, and Core Values. Only player characters have Approaches. Not even the most important of GM characters has Approaches, or Twists with which to activate them. Approaches are the province of player characters.

Activating Approaches

Each game session you start with a number of Twists equal to your character’s Import, which can be spent through one of their Approaches to alter the story. You use them to create Advantages or Complications – typically Advantages for yourself or Complications for others, though you could certainly give a fellow character an Advantage using your Twists. The stronger the effect you want, the more Twists you need to spend to create it.

To activate a particular Approach, you spend a number of Twists equal to the level of the Effect you want to create. If you want a Minor Complication, that’s 3 Twists. If you want a Major Advantage, that’s 7 Twists. See Complications and Advantages for more detail.

Your Approach activation has to fit with both the Nature itself and the Approach. If you’d like to inflict a major injury on an opponent, Communion (Close Friends) is a bad choice. War is a better Nature for that, but your descriptor has to allow it: War (Won’t Fall Down) would likewise be a bad choice.

Approach use should also be believable. Stretching the bounds of believability is fine; many stories do that. Running roughshod over everyone’s understandings of the game world is just a bad idea. Think of Approaches as bending the story around your character, not as a power that your character can access. If you use Industry (Megastructures) to create a floating mountain, saying “Why don’t we hide in that floating mountain over there?” begs the question, “Why didn’t anyone mention this massive structure before?” Using it instead to say that the entire mountain slides in from some nearby world, now that would raise some interesting questions.

Once applicability and believability have been established, Approach activation beats out all other game mechanics. It doesn’t matter whether your character is completely outclassed; Twists win.

Effects can touch multiple characters, but not groups large enough to warrant the Infrastructure descriptor. You cannot easily create large-scale Effects. Those Effects typically require creating a Plot and following it through.

Trivial Approach Activations

Once per scene you can generate a Trivial Complication or Advantage for free. For example, if our heroine is known as a famous athlete, with the Approach Communion (Loved By All), people will tend to crowd around her in public spaces. This costs no Twists – the people who do this are not adversely impacted, nor is the character gaining any real advantage.

If your GM tells you that you can’t benefit from this (as part of the GM’s plot, no doubt), you receive a Twist the first time this happens in a game session.

Taking Complications

Twists come from several different sources, as described in the “Sources of Twists” sidebar. The greatest potential source of Twists is from taking Complications. When you do this, you, the player, are allowed to describe problems that your character must overcome. You can choose any sort of Complication you like. Generally, only rough descriptions are necessary. Your GM will fill in the rest.

The level of the Complication indicates the maximum number of Twists that are available by willingly accepting the listed Complication. If your Complication is something you can mitigate by using your magic, then you subtract your Power score from the number of Twists you would receive (as per the Bad Luck Rule. On the other hand, if your problems stem from your magic itself and further use would only exacerbate things, you keep the full number of Twists (as per the Worst Enemy rule.

If you have a Core Value that aligns with the Complication that you are taking, and that CV is rated 3 or higher, you gain an extra Twist from that Complication.

When you take a Complication, you can spend the associated Twists immediately or you can “bank” them for later use any time until the end of the current session of play. Choose which Nature you store them in.

Players may not hold more than 12 twists at one time. If you are about to go over 12 Twists, you may immediately spend some of them before gaining your new set of Twists. Individual Natures can store up to 6 Twists, with additional Twists spilling over into adjacent Natures.

The GM, as part of the job of creating a story, will often be placing your character into situations similar to Complications. You receive no Twists for this. You also do not receive Twists for Complications incurred as the result of a conflict. Only Complications you accept of your own initiative provide Twists.

Spot Rules for Twists and Approaches

Plot Immunity

Spending 5 Twists will allow you to invoke “Plot Immunity” for your character. Plot Immunity is a way of saying, “I’m in danger, but I’m going to get out of it somehow.” It’s also a way of telling the GM that you’d like this particular hazard to be over and done so that you can get back to other parts of the game.

Plot Immunity must be focused through an Approach, or involve your Natures or Professions in some way. Use things that are already established about your character to get you out of the mess in some astonishing but inevitable way. Plot Immunity works best when your character is off-screen, or has an obvious solution to the problem. You might have a problem that could be taken care of in other ways, given a little legwork and some trading favors, but Plot Immunity will do it right away.

If you activate Plot Immunity, your GM cannot face you with the same hazard in the same game session. If your character decides to intentionally return to a known danger, however, your old use of Plot Immunity will not save your character this time.

The Frying Pan Rule

When dealing with a Complication that you willingly accepted, you cannot spend Twists to ameliorate its effects in any way at all.

Complications are also explicitly allowed to end the duration on existing Approach activations and bring elements removed by Plot Immunity back into play.

You may, if you wish, avoid Complications that you received as a result of Conflicts by spending Twists. Only Complications that you voluntarily took in order to get Twists are immune to Approach use.

No PVP

You cannot use Approaches to affect other characters directly with a Complication. If you create a Major Advantage for yourself that makes you rich and then stage a hostile takeover of another PCs company, that’s fine. The takeover will happen using the normal Conflict rules, at the character level. However, if you try to work at the player level and give the other PC a Complication that takes away their company, that’s not allowed. When you activate an Approach, you can only affect NPCs with your Complications. Creating an Advantage for a fellow PC is acceptable, as long as you have their approval.

Twist vs. Twist

Once a Twist has been used, it can’t be undone, even by the use of another Twist. However, players whose characters have different goals might use several Twists to wrest control of the story back and forth, each describing a particular event or series of events. They can’t contradict each other, but they are allowed to bring in new elements that mitigate the effects of the previous Twist.

Twists and Scope

Twist use is intended to have a great deal of power. It is also intended to have a relatively narrow scope. This means that the resulting Complications typically only affect a couple of people, work in the short term, or preserve the status quo

It is possible to use Twists to impose longer-lasting changes on the game world. You can do this by crafting Plots. This is an excellent use for “leftover” Twists at the end of a session, as well as being a good way for players to cooperate. Plots are not easy or quick to create, but they have the potential to completely alter a campaign.

Game vs. Story

Complications have two parts: an impact on the story (e.g. kidnapping, loss of income) and a game effect (e.g. losing access to Natures, having your Core Value diminished). Who gets to choose what those effects are?

During conflict, the player and GM (or occasionally two players) decide on both the story effect and the game effect for each other’s characters.

When intentionally accepting a Complication to gain Twists, the player may describe the story effect on his or her character for that Complication. However, the GM chooses the game effect.

Sometimes the GM or the inflicting player might choose to waive a game effect, deciding that the story impact as described warrants no effect in game terms. However, until the Complication is fixed, game effects can always be applied if they seem appropriate to the situation. Story effects should not be waived – they are the “why” of the Complication, and merely applying penalties to character attributes does not provide enough detail or make for an interesting enough story.

Sources of Twists

For easy reference, here are the major ways to gain Twists:

Bad Luck / Worst Enemy: Invoking these rules provides you Twists in a particular Nature, in exchange for a mishap. For the Bad Luck Rule, the number of Twists is equal to the level of Complication that ensues, minus your Power score, with a minimum of one. For the Worst Enemy Rule, your Power score is not subtracted. The Frying Pan Rule applies in both cases.

Core Value: If you have a CV rated at 3 or higher, you receive one extra Twist in any Nature each time you suffer a Complication related to that CV. GMs are encouraged to interpret the word “related” liberally. You do not receive this extra Twist for your Self-Preservation CV.

Unconventional Form: If you have a unconventional Earthly Form and suffer because of it, you receive Twists as per the Bad Luck Rule.

Approach Stymied: The first time during a gaming session that your GM overrules the use of an Approach for a Trivial Effect, you gain a Twist for that Nature.

Because The GM Said So: Your GM may decide to give you Twists at any time, in any Nature, for any reason.

Each Nature holds a maximum of six Twists. If you gain more, they must spill over into adjacent Natures or be lost.

Examples of Taking Complications

A renegade Hearth-kin is dragged to the nearest home-tree to face a stern talking-to by the local elder.

Their player says, “Crap, I bet I even know this person. I’m probably related to them. I was probably a jerk to them when I was a kid.”

“Do you want to make that into a Complication?” asks the GM.

“Yeah, sure!” says the player. “Just a Minor one? Like, it’s a small strike against me, not something they’ll throw me into jail for.”

“Sounds good. Meet Grandma Drought. She does not look happy to see you.”

This falls under the Bad Luck Rule. The normal award for a minor complication is 3 Twists, but this character has Power 1, so they get 2 Twists instead.

The team’s leader has been acting as a communications hub, using multiple simultaneous Farspeech connections. They find themselves going into a dangerous situation and wanting some extra Twists.

Farspeech has emotional burn-in as a potential side effect. The leader’s player activates the Bad Luck Rule, taking a Moderate complication. They state that the leader has been routing too many conversations and they’re getting paranoid, likely to treat anyone who comes into the situation as an enemy. Moderate Complications are worth 5 Twists. The leader’s Power is 2, so they get 3 Twists from this.

In the same example as above, the team’s samaritan would love to come in and help the leader. The character doesn’t realize that they’ll be attacked on sight because of the leader’s current paranoia. However, the player knows, and has some options.

Option 1: They could spend some Twists through an Approach like Mystery (Danger Sense) to avoid the situation, or Communion (Friendship) to avoid triggering the leader’s paranoia.

Option 2: They could attempt to use Natures like Trickery or Mystery (probably with Spy or Criminal) to stay hidden, helping the leader from a distance. This would require a short conflict between the samaritan and the leader.

Option 3: They could take a Complication to represent an injury: they show up to help only to be immediately blasted by the leader.

A Glimmermere resident has been using the Shell Game to adjust the aspect of a local ley line. One of the side effects of the Shell Game is chaotic flow. The artist’s player decides that traces of the many different aspects the artist handled stick with them, causing difficulty in spellcasting and resulting in a number of wild effects any time they use an Art.

The player selects a Major Complication: high impact and long-lasting. Because this sort of complication can’t be addressed by the character themselves and their use of magic, it’s governed by the Worst Enemy rule rather than the Bad Luck Rule. They get the full 7 Twists regardless of their Power score – which is good, because they’re gonna need some help.

The GM has created a minor antagonist that’s unexpectedly popular with the players. The team loves to hate them. One of the players takes a Major Complication to ensure that the antagonist constantly has information on where they’re going to be and what their plans are. Another one takes a Moderate Complication stating that the antagonist is secretly in love with their character, which could certainly cause trouble. The GM crosses out “lackey” on the NPC’s character sheet and replaces it with “nemesis”.

The player of a Hearth-kin defender wants to take a Moderate complication for a broken arm at the end of a fight, and then use regeneration (without spending any Reserve) to heal that break before anything else happens. The GM denies this Complication under the Big Deal rule: if your Complication doesn’t affect you, it’s not a Complication.

Examples of Twist Use

A Sovereign warlord (played by the GM) activates the Ten-Crore Stones of Death, vaporizing the area where an Ægis Shieldbearer (a player character) was attempting to hide from them. This triggers the Obligatory Instant Death Cutscene Rule, giving the player a chance to spend Twists. The player spends three Twists through the Shieldbearer’s Trickery (Right Behind You) Approach, selecting an “escaped but not without injury” advantage. The Shieldbearer activates a risky, painful teleportation art, evading the Ten-Crore Stones’ effect, and closing in to engage the Sovereign in hand-to-hand combat.

A dream-pathogen sweeps through the city, infecting the team and threatening to put everyone into an eternal sleep. Most of them have enough Self to shrug it off, but their Returner sidekick is in trouble.

Their player would like to activate Plot Immunity. Their Approach for the Mystery-nature is “Genius Through Stupidity”. The player declares that the disease relies on a mystical experience of the world that their Returner simply doesn’t have. It passes over them harmlessly.

This would cost 5 Twists from Mystery or an adjacent Nature. Right now they’ve got 3 in Mystery and 1 in Trickery, so they need 1 more. With Power 0, they can take a Trivial Complication to get the Twist. (assuming they haven’t done so yet this session). The Returner yawns and stretches and then looks around confused as everyone else on the street falls down.

A Geometer loiters outside their club’s headquarters, trying to overhear the plans for an upcoming social event. They could spend Twists through an Approach like Mystery (Eavesdropper) or Mystery (Silent Observer). How big of an effect they need to create depends on exactly what they want to achieve. If they want to know the current plans and take their chances that they might change, that’s a moderate advantage – accurate information, short-term. If the player wants to lock in those plans so they don’t change, they could spend a Shift to bump it up to a major advantage for long-term information.

How might you use Twists to stop a massive volcanic eruption?

If there’s no one in danger, it’s probably a Minor Advantage. It might feel strange to say there’s no real impact when a mountain explodes, but volcanoes erupt every day without being an important part of anyone’s story.

Plot Immunity could save your character (and probably a few others nearby), but it wouldn’t stop the lava.

If you need to buy time for locals to evacuate, that’s Moderate, granting you a short-term Advantage with key information. Of course, if the eruption is happening right now, you might not get enough time from that.

If you intend to save a village with a massive, flamboyant magical effect (e.g. transmuting everyone inside it to a fire elemental, freezing the lava solid, etc.), that’s a Major advantage. It’s high impact and long-lasting.

Regardless of what level of Advantage is required, you’ll need an appropriate Approach. Good examples include Mystery (Prediction), War (Violence Turned Back), Industry (Instant Construction), Industry (Invulnerability), Mystery (Elemental Savant), and many others.

An Ægis shieldbearer wants to comfort a crying child, but they’re an enormous, armored, photon-winged, gold-clawed bringer of death. That’s a bit of a barrier. However, the shieldbearer has Communion (Trusting Shoulder) and a few Twists to spend. The GM rules that this is not a major story point, and the shieldbearer can comfort the child and win the trust of their family with a Minor effect.

Plots

Plots are an extension of normal Approach use. They are a way to spend Twists, sometimes dozens of them, over a long period of time, in order to create significant changes in the game world.

In order to create a Plot, all three of the following criteria must be met:

The events and resolution of the Plot must be related to one or more of your character’s Approaches.

At least one of your character’s Core Values must be related to the Plot. Ideally, that CV would be either reaffirmed or eroded by the completion of the Plot.

Your character must somehow be involved. This may be directly (as an instigator), indirectly (through support), or even as a bystander swept up in the events. You cannot merely benefit from the Plot, you must be there for it.

If those criteria are met, you may spend Twists over the course of multiple game sessions in order to create a Complication or Advantage from the Large-Scale Effects list.

You can spend Twists at any time to add to add to the Plot’s total. You should keep a running tally of the number of points spent. Each time, note which Approach and which Core Value are involved, as well as the number of Twists spent. This will help to shape the path of the Plot, which is evolving behind the scenes as the game progresses.

Multiple characters may spend Twists to speed a Plot to its resolution. One character will be the “lead” for a particular Plot during each session, and is allowed to spend as many Twists as he or she desires. Other players who are involved must still have their characters meet the criteria, but may only spend one Twist each per session.

The desired resolution of a Plot must be described from the beginning. Changing the course of a Plot is possible, but the Plot will lose half of the Twists currently invested in it.

GM Action on Plots

GMs can add Twists to existing Plots as well. Each time the players willingly take a Complication, you can choose to give that player an extra Twist, and also add a Twist to a particular Plot. The Complication doesn’t need to be directly related to the Plot at hand, but the character must be somehow involved.

The Plot Veto

Ordinary Twist expenditures are not subject to GM approval. Unless the group considers your Effect to be completely outside the realm of believability, Twists are the most effective and powerful tools in the game.

This works partly because most Effects are of short duration. They are fairly personal, and almost never have consequences beyond the end of the current game session. Large-scale effects, on the other hand, can affect thousands or even millions of people for the rest of their lives, and can change the landscape of the game.

The GM’s job in Sorcerously Advanced is to create a storyline for the players in which their characters can be involved, and which is entertaining for all. In general we find that most player-level Plots don’t interfere with the game’s main storyline. These stories can coexist peacefully, metaphorically speaking.

However, there may come a time when a player’s Plot runs directly counter to the story that the GM is attempting to tell. For instance, the GM may have the Fellowship of Truth as a major villain in the game, and desire to use them as an organization many times until the players’ characters finally defeat them. One of the players, whether aware of this storyline or not, might want to destroy the Fellowship using a Plot. This would shortcut much of the GM’s planning and hard work. Such things can be frustrating for the GM and lead to a game in which the GM is not as well-prepared and the party doesn’t have as much fun.

There may also be times when a Plot would weaken or cheapen the GM’s story, or the GM’s planned story would do the same to a player’s Plot.

In such situations, the GM can veto a Plot, or ask that it be delayed until after a certain event. This veto power should be used immediately when the Plot is created, and any Twists spent on the Plot should be refunded.

We recommend that this veto be used as rarely as possible. Sorc is a game with significant GM authority, but the players should also be given as much agency as the game will allow. Such Plots might even lead to a burst of inspiration and creativity for the GM that improves the game! Sometimes it’s worth trying to figure out how both stories can occur. Negotiating a possible compromise is a much better option than a flat-out veto.

GM Plot Seeds

Plots can also be a great way for the GM to tell the players where the game is going, and invite them to pace the sessions. Players can then direct the game towards things in which they are interested, and the GM will know what plots the players are interested in pursuing.

This is as easy as writing up a sheet of paper with a set of “Plot Seeds”. Each one has an end goal and a Twist amount that would be required to reach it. For instance, one might say “Root out corruption in the Guild leadership,” with a cost of 20 Twists. As players spend more Twists on that, the GM introduces elements of that particular plot. If no players are interested in pursuing that Plot, it will remain untouched and the GM will know to focus effort elsewhere.

Plot Seeds are an optional item, and even if you use them, not every storyline the GM wants to pursue needs to be a Plot Seed (or two or three).

The GM can still introduce stories normally. Plot Seeds are more like an official feedback channel between the GM and players that helps both sides influence the flow of the game.

Example Plots

Corruption in the Archive

You’re a cynical player, and you don’t believe that the Archive could be on the level. There must be something sinister going on behind the scenes.

You create a Plot that will end with a Moderate Complication for the Archive, publicly revealing the corruption of a highly-ranked Archivist. The Archive is a Society with Infrastructure II. This Plot will cost 5 for the Moderate Complication, times 3 for Infrastructure II: 15 Twists. The GM approves the Plot, and quietly notes a few of the NPCs the players know who might be in on the conspiracy.

The Second Death

You are the GM. In your game, there’s a flaw in the Great Enchantment of the Cult of the Empty Grave. If it’s not fixed, ghosts affected by the Great Enchantment will die of old age. This will cause panic in the Cult, as it splinters into smaller organizations, some pressing on without their ancestors, others working to constantly restore them.

You put this on the table as follows: “Critical Complication: the Cult’s ghosts can die of old age.” You don’t need to describe any of the repercussions of this event. The players can then choose whether to enable this event to pass, assuming their characters meet the criteria: it’s related to one of their Approaches, it’s related to one of their Core Values, and they’re directly involved in discovering this or being impacted by it. As the GM, you can also add a Twist to this Plot when characters who are involved with it take a voluntary Complication.

Farewell to Fellowship

You’re sick of the Fellowship of Truth. Just so completely fed up with everything about them. You decide to remove them from the game world.

You declare that the majority of the Fellowship is going to try to tap directly into Aion for power and be vaporized in the process. That’s a Critical Complication for 9 Twists, times 3 for an Infrastructure II group like the Fellowship, for a total of 27 Twists. There may be a handful of Fellowship members still alive afterward, but for all intents and purposes the group will be gone.

As a card-carrying Archivist, your “Knowledge for the Sake of the World” CV ties in well here. You also have a Self (Strong Soul) Approach that gives you an idea: You request that the GM make you a bystander in their attempts, perhaps hoping to sacrifice you to power their magic. The GM was planning on having a big confrontation between your group and the Fellowship, but honestly having their own hubris destroy them sounds just fine, so they approve the Plot. You save up some Twists so you can get kidnapped and watch the fireworks.

Diadem Declares War

You’re playing a Diadem merchant. The GM has a side plot wherein your character’s newborn grandchild has been kidnapped by the Unroyal. Rather than run off to handle this yourself, you decide to create a Plot instead: you’re going to call in favors, drum up support, and convince Diadem to officially declare war on the Unroyal.

Your character has Communion (Bonds of Love) and a Might Makes Right CV. Their status as a close relative of the kidnapping victim definitely qualifies them as “somehow involved”. All three criteria are met. The GM agrees and approves the Plot.

You call this a Minor Complication for the Unroyal, stating that they would rather punish the kidnapper than avoid war. A Minor Complication is 3 Twists, times 4 for an Infrastructure III group like the Unroyal. The total cost is 12 Twists.

You can contribute as many Twists per session as you like. Other players whose characters meet the criteria may contribute one Twist per session if they like.

You’d like your character the become the mayor of a city, to set up some future stories. The GM suggests that this should be personal-level Twist use rather than a Plot, since it doesn’t necessarily change the city all that much.

You like the idea of the Basement Lines, and want to make a Plot that says the Groundbreakers are successful in their search for them. The GM asks you what you want as ramifications for this plot. The two of you brainstorm, and you catch on to the idea of the Groundbreakers becoming a full civilization and a major rival to Those Above. Adding a full level of Infrastructure to a group is definitely a Critical Advantage, totaling 27 Twists. You’re eager to get started, but the GM points out that you don’t meet the criteria yet – your character has nothing to do with either the Groundbreakers or Those Above. They suggest that you look for places where Becoming or some individual-level Twist use could tie you into one of those groups.

Existentialism

Because life can get complicated for your characters, there are certain rules regarding what does and doesn’t count as “your character”. You may end up getting cursed to sleep forever, incinerated and resurrected, replaced with a mirror-universe duplicate, and worse. When does that remove your ability to play your character?

Don’t Skip This Section

This section answers key questions about sweeping magical changes to your character, and the limits of what you can do with your character. If you’re playing a hard, fun, high-magic game with dangerous events, you will run into questions like “How much can I shapeshift my own brain before I’m no longer actually me?”

The title of this section might remind you more of philosophy than sorcery. That’s ok. These are philosophical questions. If you don’t like the answers, you can change them. The Advice chapter will tell you how.

The Rule of Intent

If something happens to your character as an intentional result of your character’s actions, you control the resulting character. If you curse yourself, shapeshift, have a frontal lobotomy, or even kill yourself so you can be resurrected later, you still play the resulting character even if one could argue that it “isn’t still you”. The guiding principle at work is that characters are defined by their choices.

The Rule of Force

In the case of drastic changes to your character that your character would have opposed, knowing the outcome, you or the GM may decide that you no longer control the resulting character. If you get forcibly shapeshifted, cursed, brainwashed, etc. into something your old character wouldn’t have agreed with, the default is that it’s time to pick up a new character. The old one is now an NPC. You and the GM can talk about it if you still want to play the resulting character. If either one of you says “no,” then it’s time to make a new one.

The Rule of Identity

All versions and instances of your character are still yours to control unless the Rule of Force comes into play. If you intentionally make 100 clones of yourself, you have authority over all of them. If someone made a clone of you without your knowledge, things would depends on whether your character would appreciate that or not, as per the Rule of Force.

The Rule of Reversals

If the drastic changes mentioned in the Rule of Force are substantially reversed, you may, at your option, regain control of your character.

The Rule of Ontological Inertia

If a character leaves play, permanently or temporarily, the player can still activate Approaches from the character until the player receives a new character or the old one returns to play. This primarily applies to dead or unconscious characters, but can also apply to people who are stuck in the Red Space or trapped in unbreakable crystal. Such characters are unlikely to garner Twists except at the beginning of a session, so playing a permanently dead character rather limits one’s options.

The Guideline of Equal Play

One item that didn’t quite make it to “rule” status is the Guideline of Equal Play. In Sorc there are many ways to multiply the effectiveness of a character. If the party splits, creating a mirror-duplicate may allow certain characters to follow both groups at once, or even split into multiple parties all by themselves. However, due to the need to share play time with other players, a player who has split their character should expect that some actions will be glossed over. A 100-body one-man police force is great, but its player won’t be roleplaying each of them individually.

Infrastructure

The rules of Sorcerously Advanced are intended for human-scale characters and conflicts, but they can also handle events on the scale of cities, societies, or even nations. Because of their interconnected nature and need for support, Sorc denotes these groups with the Infrastructure descriptor, which has three levels:

  1. Armadas, cities, thousands of people working together or tens of thousands with weak interconnection.
  2. Societies, provinces and nations, armies, millions of people working together or tens of millions with weak interconnection.
  3. Civilizations: multiple nations working together on one or many continents.

The Infrastructure descriptor is a particularly important one, as it has substantial game effects. These are collected below for easy reference.

Large-scale Characters

Characters built with the Infrastructure descriptor (such as automata swarms, living skyships, or a horde of mirror duplicates) increase their Power score by 1 in every Tradition. This has the usual side-effect of reducing their Import by 1.

You cannot play a character with more than one level of Infrastructure. Nature scores are still capped at level 5.

Cross-Level Conflicts

In a conflict between groups with differing levels of Infrastructure, the larger group applies the maximum Teamwork bonus for the type of conflict involved: +4 for most conflicts, +6 for highly parallel ones (see Teamwork in Conflicts).

If the larger group would suffer a Complication, the level of that Complication is reduced by one step for each level of Infrastructure. If the smaller group also has Infrastructure, use the difference in levels to find the reduction. This reduction also applies to any Advantage the smaller party might try to gain.

Rituals for Large Groups

Rituals can affect groups with Infrastructure. Infrastructure I adds 2 time steps, II adds 4 time steps, and III adds 6 time steps. You may also need a successful large-scale Conflict to pull off the project, as most large groups are well-defended.

If you yourself have the Infrastructure descriptor, treat this as a +4 Teamwork modifier in Rituals.

As the Great Basin is particularly immense, and some magical workings are known to affect the entirety of it (and beyond), there are three “virtual infrastructure” levels that represent affecting entire worlds with a magical working:

  1. Magical works that affect multiple civilizations at once. +8 time steps
  2. Complex and vast rituals that change the nature of the entire Basin. +10 time steps
  3. Sorcerous superstructures that touch upon all worlds in the Kaleidoscope and beyond. +12 time shifts

There are no actual groups that possess this level of infrastructure. These levels only exist for the purpose of truly vast magical efforts like building the Travel Lattice.

Approaches with a Cast of Thousands

Groups with Infrastructure cost more to affect with Approach use. See below for the exact numbers. Because of the 12-Twist cap, ordinary Approach use typically cannot affect large groups, and you will need to use Plots instead.

Self-affecting Twist use (such as Plot Immunity) is not more expensive for characters with Infrastructure.

Nature Scores for Large Groups

Sorcerously Advanced uses Nature scores to describe the personal prowess of individual characters. We also use a similar set of Natures to describe the strengths and weaknesses of large organizations.

Organizations and characters with Infrastructure I still have the usual six Nature scores. Above that, things change a bit.

For groups with Infrastructure II or III, the Inudstry-nature is less about creating larger or more powerful things, and more about trade, resources, and flow. The Self of a large organization speaks to its internal cohesion, its resilience, and the way it knows and stands by its Core Values. It also protects the bodies and minds of its citizens. War is perhaps the least-changed Nature, still representing destructive power and strategic acumen.

However, organizations with Infrastructure II or III no longer have Mystery, Communion, and Trickery ratings. Instead, we describe them in terms of Statecraft, Tradecraft, and Research.

Statecraft is diplomacy, effective foreign policy, and the ability to make advantageous treaties with other large organizations. People who contribute to a group’s Statecraft rating usually employ a combination of Communion and Trickery, though they may lean toward one or the other.

Tradecraft is spy-work, espionage, and generally collecting “intelligence” on one’s rivals. On the micro level it is accomplished through Trickery, Mystery, or a combination of the two.

Research moves the arts of sorcery forward, pushing toward the day when magic becomes technology. Groups with many high-Mystery individuals tend to lean on them; others use Communion to build research alliances and share knowledge.

The Nature scores of an Infrastructure II or III organization have no bearing on the attributes of individuals. They’re good estimates for average citizen Nature scores, but some people will have much higher or lower scores.

Infrastructure-level Magic

So what does it mean when a city has a high Industry rating? What does a country’s Tradecraft represent? Here’s a broad overview of what each Nature accomplishes at the large scale.

Industry: More durable civic structures. Improved weather. Better logistics. Better-tasting food. A more mobile populace with faster transit. Clean and well-conditioned flow. Restrictions on the use of Travel. The chronal terraforming of the Worldbuilders.

Research: More magic use in everyday life. Outposts in the Worlds Beyond and the Dream Realms, or even the empty space beyond the Basin. Influential research institutions. A wider variety of Arts, some of which have been expanded or enhanced.

Self: Well-supported healers and respected therapists. Education that teaches more effectively. The ability to settle harsh environments. The ability to slowly change Core Values or their ratings. The creation of the Golden Ægis’ Overmind.

Statecraft: Subtle enhancements of charisma and persuasiveness. Carefully crafted laws. Improved civic planning. Precognitive examination of how alliances will unfold. More treaties and fewer wars.

Tradecraft: Farspeech networks. Secret partnerships with spirit courts. A populace that reports suspicious events to the authorities. More broken treaties.

War: Faster responses to incursions. Better-armed militaries. The Threefold Gates of Retribution that protect large settlements from outside magical assault. Influential generals. More wars.

Large-Scale Effects

Just as individual characters can suffer Complications, so too can cities, societies, or entire civilizations. When two large groups face off against each other, or when the Plot system is in use, use this set of Effects.

For the most part, a civilization-level conflict plays out like a character-level conflict. However, the Effects listed for regular human beings are not quite appropriate for large groups with infrastructure.

As with individual-level Effects, the numbers in parentheses are used to rate the severity of the Complication or Advantage. The slashes separate costs for groups of Infrastructure I, II, or III. If you prefer a formula, start with the Twist cost for an ordinary Complication and multiply by the level of Infrastructure for that group plus one. For instance, creating a Minor (3) leak of classified information from a Society (typically Infrastructure II) would cost 3 x (2+1) = 9 Twists.

Because of the sheer number of Twists required, and the 12-Twist cap for individual characters, it is typically impossible to buy this kind of Effect with a one-time Twist expenditure. Instead, use the Plot rules to generate long-term changes in the game world.

Trivial (2/3/4): Trivial Complications involve immediate and minor impact. Government officials annoyed or distracted. Lose track of a single being who is not being actively followed. A civ-wide CV is challenged publicly. Minor ley line fracture. A handful of casualties.

Minor (6/9/12): Minor Complications are short- term, low-impact, and involve skeletal information. They can be annoying if not addressed (or if several build up), but they are unlikely to be a problem in the short term. An opposing group successfully disengages from a long-term conflict, though not without losses. Slightly delayed in your plans. Hundreds or thousands of casualties. Government officials likely refer to this event as a “national tragedy”. Classified information is revealed to a group currently neutral to you. Minor but noticeable monetary loss.

Moderate (10/15/20): Moderate Complications are long-term or high-impact, but not both. A single high-level political campaign is decided in a particular direction. Major classified information revealed to the enemy. Tens of thousands of casualties. Lose control of a town. Join an unwise alliance. An agency’s plans are significantly delayed. Substantial economic losses or market crash. Opposing group disengages conflict without significant harm.

Major (14/21/28): Major Complications are both long-term and high-impact. Complications at this level can remove a level of the Infrastructure descriptor. Intelligence services return significant incorrect results. Citizens convinced of a particular fact or falsehood. Millions of casualties. Important state secrets revealed to the enemy. Lose control of a major city. Martial law declared or revoked for a substantial part of the populace. Other groups turn against you, though not your closest allies. A Society rejects the entire civilization. Government ignores wisdom from its advisors. A Core Value is reinterpreted.

Critical (18/27/36): Collapse of the civilization or worse. Complications at this level may bring the Rule of Force into play. Over half of the population brainwashed, dying, or near death. A Core Value is removed or changed. Government living in exile. The group’s peoples are driven from their homelands. Economy, military, or intelligence agencies fall completely under external controls. Allies turned against you. Long-term plans ruined. This condition does not fade quickly or easily.
